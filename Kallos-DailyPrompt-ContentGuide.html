Daily Prompt Content Guide
What a Daily Prompt is, what belongs in each field, how to write each section, and fully worked examples. Read this before creating prompts in Sanity Studio.

What is a Daily Prompt

A Daily Prompt is a standalone daily practice — not a lesson, not a chapter in a Journey. It is a single encounter with something beautiful, paired with a question that opens a door. The user may spend 3 minutes with it or 30. They set the pace.

It is delivered as a scrollable immersive page. The content piece (artwork, landscape, music, literature) is the anchor. Everything else — the question, the reading, the music, the action — serves the encounter with that single thing.

The Daily Prompt is an invitation, not a curriculum. It should feel like someone leaving a beautiful thing on your doorstep with a note — not a teacher waiting for your answer.

The audience is the non-Catholic curious seeker. They are moved by beauty and asking deeper questions. They may not know who Aquinas is. They may have never been inside a basilica. Write for someone intelligent and open, not someone already fluent in Catholic tradition.

The 5 Founding Themes

Daily prompts do not have to follow a theme, but grounding them in one of the five founding themes gives editorial coherence over time and creates a natural connection to the Journeys.

Light
"What does light reveal that darkness hides?"
Silence
"What do we hear when we stop talking?"
Suffering & Beauty
"How does beauty survive darkness?"
Creation
"What is the world trying to tell us?"
Home / The Restless Heart
"Why do we long for something we've never seen?"

Content Types You Can Feature

Any of the 7 content types can anchor a Daily Prompt. Vary them across the week — not every prompt needs to be a painting.

Sacred Art

Paintings, sculptures, icons, frescoes, churches. Caravaggio, Rublev, Fra Angelico, Bernini.

Thinker

Plato, Augustine, Aquinas, C.S. Lewis, von Balthasar, Roger Scruton. One idea, not a lecture.

Literature

Hopkins, Rilke, Dante, Dostoevsky, Tolkien. A poem, a passage, a single image from a novel.

Music

Gregorian chant, Arvo Pärt, Bach, Palestrina, Tavener. Let the music be the object of attention.

Food & Wine

Cistercian monasteries, 3-generation bakeries, natural wine, the sacred in the everyday.

Landscape

Creation theology, places where the world speaks. Mountains, light over water, ancient forests.

Watch / Listen

YouTube, documentary, lecture, podcast. Something to encounter rather than just read.

Fields — What Goes Where

Date Required
The date this prompt appears. One prompt per day. Format: YYYY-MM-DD.
Build a bank of prompts in advance — aim for at least 2 weeks ahead. Do not leave today's date empty the morning of.
Featured Content Required
The content item this prompt is built around. Must already exist in Sanity as a Content Item. The item's image becomes the hero image for the full-screen prompt experience.
The content item needs a high-resolution image (min 1200px). The image is the first thing the user sees — it carries significant weight. Choose images with enough tonal depth for the dark espresso treatment.
Prompt Question Required
The single reflection question for the day. This is the heart of the prompt — the door the image opens.
One question only. Under 20 words ideally. A question that has no obvious answer. It should create space, not direct traffic. Never start with "How do you..." — start with "What..." or "Where..." or "When..."
Curator Note Optional
The human editorial voice. Why you chose this today. What you notice in it. Not an explanation of the content — a personal aside.
Write in first person. Keep it to 2–3 sentences. This is the note slipped under the door, not the lecture notes. If you have nothing genuine to say, leave it blank — forced notes read as forced.
Reading (Lectio) Optional
Lectio — "reading" or "word"
A short quote, scripture passage, or text that deepens the encounter with the content. Not an explanation — a resonance. The text should be able to stand alone as beautiful.
Keep it short — 2 to 5 sentences maximum. If it needs context to make sense, it is the wrong text. Include attribution: author name, work title, and where relevant, chapter/verse. Scripture is appropriate but not required — poetry, philosophy, and literature belong here equally.
Music (Auditio) Optional
Auditio — "hearing" or "listening"
A piece of music to accompany the practice. If no external link is provided, the app defaults to the built-in chant or ambient player. If a specific piece is curated, provide a Spotify or YouTube link.
The music should be non-verbal or in a language the user doesn't need to understand — chant, classical, ambient, sacred polyphony. Music with lyrics in English will compete with the reading and the actio. Not every prompt needs a specific piece — the built-in chant is always available and always appropriate.
Action (Actio) Optional — but strongly encouraged
Actio — "action" or "doing"
An invitation to carry one thing from the practice into the day. One concrete, gentle action. Not a homework assignment — a small opening.
One sentence. Concrete but not prescriptive — "Notice one place where light falls differently today" not "Go outside and look at the sky for ten minutes." Should feel possible for a person in any circumstance — at a desk, on a train, in a meeting. This is also the section that triggers the daily streak — the user is considered to have completed the practice when they scroll to this section.
Related Journey Optional
If one of the 5 Journeys deepens what this prompt opens, link it here. A quiet CTA appears at the bottom of the prompt page: "Go deeper · [Journey name]"
Only link a Journey if the connection is genuine and obvious to the user, not just thematically adjacent. A Light prompt that directly introduces themes from the Light Journey is a good link. A vague thematic similarity is not.
Theme Optional
Tags this prompt to one of the 5 founding themes. Helps users see the connection between a Daily Prompt and a Journey. Not visible to users directly — used for filtering and future features.
Tag it if the connection is clear. Leave it blank if the prompt is more general.

Writing the Prompt Question

The prompt question is the hardest thing to write well. Here is what separates a good one from a weak one.

Do Questions that open space and have no single answer.

"What do you notice first — what the light touches, or what it doesn't?"

"Where in this does something familiar become strange?"

"What is the painter asking you to feel before you understand?"

"What would it mean to belong to a place like this?"
Don't Questions that direct the user toward a predetermined answer.

"How does this painting show God's love for us?"

"What does this remind you of from your childhood?"

"Can you see the Holy Spirit in this image?"

"How does this make you feel about beauty?"

The question should be answerable by a person who has never been to church and equally rich for someone who has. If it requires theological background to feel the weight of it, rewrite it.

  • Does the question arise genuinely from the content piece, or could it be attached to anything?
  • Is there a real answer you don't know? Or are you guiding the user toward your answer?
  • Can someone who is not religious engage with it fully?
  • Is it under 20 words?
  • Would you want to sit with this question yourself?

Writing the Actio

The Actio is a single small act — something the user can do today, in ordinary life, that keeps the encounter alive. It is not a spiritual exercise. It is not a task. It is a direction of attention.

Do "Notice one place today where you find yourself drawn toward silence."

"Carry one image from this painting with you — let it surface when you least expect it."

"Find one moment before tonight when you stop and look at the sky for thirty seconds."

"Let one meal today be eaten slowly, without your phone."
Don't "Pray a rosary in thanksgiving for the beauty of this artwork."

"Read the full passage from which this quote is taken."

"Share this with someone you love today."

"Journal for 10 minutes about what you noticed in this practice."

The action should be completable without any preparation, materials, or religious knowledge. The test: could a non-practicing person in a busy office building do this today? If yes, it's right.

Editorial Rhythm — Planning Across the Week

Vary the content types across the week so the experience doesn't become predictable. A suggested rhythm — not a rule:

Day Content Type Notes
Monday Sacred Art or Landscape Visual anchor to start the week. Something to hold in the mind.
Tuesday Literature or Thinker A single idea or image from text. Shorter is better mid-week.
Wednesday Music Music as the primary content — let it be the object of attention, not background.
Thursday Sacred Art or Thinker Depth — something that rewards more than a quick glance.
Friday Landscape or Watch/Listen Outward gaze toward the weekend. Creation, place, the world.
Saturday Food & Wine or Literature Slower pace. The sacred in the everyday. Something domestic.
Sunday Sacred Art or Landscape The quietest, most contemplative day. An image to sit inside.

Aim to connect 1–2 prompts per week to an active Journey, giving users who are mid-Journey a natural touchpoint.

Fully Worked Examples

Three complete prompts written to the standard. Use these as templates for tone, scale, and proportion.

Example 1 — Sacred Art / Light Theme
Caravaggio: The Calling of Saint Matthew
Content Item
Caravaggio, The Calling of Saint Matthew, 1599–1600. Contarelli Chapel, Rome.
Prompt Question
Where does the light fall — and who in this painting has not yet looked up?
Curator Note
I keep returning to the hand. It could be pointing at Matthew — or deflecting. Caravaggio never makes it easy to know if the person being called wants to be found.
Reading (Lectio)
"The light shines in the darkness, and the darkness has not overcome it."
— John 1:5
Music (Auditio)
Allegri, Miserere — Tenebrae Choir
Action (Actio)
Notice one moment today when light enters a room and changes it. Don't move toward it — just notice it from where you are.
Example 2 — Landscape / Creation Theme
The Stubble Fields of the Camargue at Dawn
Content Item
Landscape content item — Camargue wetlands, southern France, early morning.
Prompt Question
What is the world doing when no one is watching it?
Curator Note
This photograph was taken 40 minutes before sunrise. The colour exists for perhaps four minutes each morning, regardless of whether anyone is there to see it. That seems important.
Reading (Lectio)
"The world is charged with the grandeur of God. It will flame out, like shining from shook foil."
— Gerard Manley Hopkins, God's Grandeur, 1877
Music (Auditio)
Built-in ambient — no specific piece required
Action (Actio)
Before you go inside today, stop outside for thirty seconds. Don't take a photo. Let the light land on your face.
Example 3 — Literature / Silence Theme
Rilke: You Must Change Your Life
Content Item
Literature content item — Rainer Maria Rilke, Archaic Torso of Apollo, 1908.
Prompt Question
When has something beautiful ever made a demand on you?
Curator Note
The last line of this poem is one of the most astonishing endings in the Western canon. It comes out of nowhere and lands like a verdict. I think Rilke was right that beauty doesn't just invite — it requires.
Reading (Lectio)
"We cannot know his legendary head with eyes like ripening fruit. And yet his torso is still suffused with brilliance from inside... for here there is no place that does not see you. You must change your life."
— Rainer Maria Rilke, Archaic Torso of Apollo, trans. Stephen Mitchell, 1908
Music (Auditio)
Arvo Pärt, Spiegel im Spiegel — Vladimir Spivakov
Action (Actio)
Carry Rilke's last line with you today: "You must change your life." Let it be a question, not a command. What does it point at?

Pre-Publish Checklist

  • Is the date correct and does the content item exist in Sanity?
  • Is the prompt question under 20 words and genuinely open?
  • Does the question work for someone with no religious background?
  • Is the Lectio short enough to read in one breath?
  • Is the Actio one sentence, concrete, and doable anywhere?
  • Does the curator note sound like a person, not a caption?
  • Is the content item image high-resolution (min 1200px)?
  • If a Journey is linked, is the connection obvious to a first-time user?
