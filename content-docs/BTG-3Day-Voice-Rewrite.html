Beauty, Truth & Goodness

3-Day Journey · Voice Rewrite · Sanity Edit Guide
These three days introduce the Transcendentals to users who have never heard the term. The goal is not to explain what they are — it is to show the user what they already know about them, and have known for a long time.
Journey Description Card — paste into Sanity journey record

Three things have been stopping people mid-sentence since before Plato had words for them. C.S. Lewis followed one of them for thirty years before he understood what it was pointing at. Three days. One thread.

This is what users see before they start the journey. Lewis's register: names the experience before the concept, promises discovery not explanation, leaves them slightly curious rather than fully oriented. Do not list the three days or explain the arc.
The Arc — internal reference

Day 1 is the feeling. Something moved you — you could not name it, and it refused to fully satisfy you. Lewis spent thirty years following that thread. He called it Joy.

Day 2 is the question that feeling opens. Beauty is not just pleasant. It has a radiance that points past itself toward something real. Aquinas called this splendor veri — the radiance of truth. He stopped writing mid-sentence when he finally saw it.

Day 3 is the arrival. Two disciples walked seven miles on a road from Jerusalem beside the answer to the question they had been asking. They did not recognize it until dinner.

The voice throughout: specific over general. Story before concept. The user arrives at the idea — it is never announced. TTS note: copy is written for both eye and ear. Every field Sheri enters into Sanity is also the TTS script. Read each field aloud before finalising.

Opening Text
Current — replace

You came here because something moved you. A painting in a museum. A line of poetry. A piece of music. The beauty of a place. Something you couldn't quite name, but it mattered. That feeling is not an accident. It is an invitation.

Revised — paste into Sanity

C.S. Lewis was eight years old when he read a line in a poem about Norse mythology. He could not have told you what the poem meant. He only knew that it made him ache for something he couldn't name.

He spent the next thirty years chasing that feeling. He followed it into music, into landscape, into other people's poetry. Each time it moved him, it also refused to satisfy him fully. Like following a scent you can never trace to its source.

He eventually concluded that this was the point. The longing itself was the message.

You don't have to agree with what he concluded. But you probably know the feeling.

Opens with a specific person in a specific moment — age eight, a Norse poem, an unnamed ache — rather than a list of generic experiences the user has already categorized. The user arrives at the concept instead of being handed it.
Curator Note / Description
Current — replace

C.S. Lewis called it "joy" — not happiness, but a kind of longing that comes when beauty touches us. It's the feeling you get when a song unexpectedly moves you to tears. When you turn a corner in a city and time stops. When you read a line someone wrote centuries ago and feel completely understood. This feeling is not sentimental. It is one of the most real things we experience. And it tells you something crucial: you are capable of encountering something beyond yourself.

Revised — paste into Sanity

He called it "Joy" with a capital letter, to distinguish it from ordinary happiness. Happiness was contentment. Joy was different: a sudden sharp longing, arriving without warning, pointing toward something it could never provide.

He found it in a toy garden his brother had made from a biscuit tin lid — moss and small stones arranged inside it. He found it in a particular kind of autumn light. He found it in that Norse poem at eight. And he discovered, over decades, that every time he reached for the thing Joy seemed to be pointing at, the Joy vanished — and whatever he had grabbed wasn't it.

What he eventually understood: Joy was not pointing at any earthly thing. It was pointing past all of them.

Specific details — biscuit tin lid, moss and stones, autumn light — do the work that the abstract paragraph was trying to do. The user experiences the concept rather than reading its definition.

Opening Text
Current — replace

At some point, every genuine beauty asks the same silent question: "Where do I come from? What does it mean that I exist?"

Revised — paste into Sanity

In 1600, Caravaggio completed an altarpiece for the French church in Rome. His patrons expected saints to look like saints. His Matthew looked like a laborer. His light came from sources you couldn't see.

The Calling of Saint Matthew shows a man sitting in a dark corner counting money at a tax collector's table. Light enters from outside the frame — from a source entirely off-canvas. It cuts across the room at an impossible angle. A hand points. Matthew looks up, startled, and touches his own chest as if asking: me?

The abstract thesis question ("Where do I come from?") is the meaning the painting carries. The painting itself is more arresting than any statement of that meaning. Show the painting; let the user feel the question rather than read it.
Curator Note / Description
Current — replace

Thomas Aquinas, the great medieval theologian, defined truth this simply: it is the correspondence between the mind and reality. The painting is beautiful because it corresponds to something real about calling, about light, about transformation. It's not making something up. It's showing us what is actually true about how love works. And Aquinas understood something crucial: all genuine beauty is a form of truth. When something is truly beautiful, it is because it reveals something real.

Revised — paste into Sanity

Thomas Aquinas was the strangest child his monastery had ever received. His father sent him to Monte Cassino at age five. The monks watched him grow into a boy who barely spoke and appeared to be working on one question for years at a time. His classmates at university called him "the dumb ox."

He became one of the most influential philosophers of the medieval world. In the last seven years of his life, he wrote the Summa Theologiae — a synthesis of everything he understood about God, beauty, truth, and how they connect. He never finished it. On December 6, 1273, during Mass, something happened. He set down his pen and said: "All that I have written seems to me like straw compared to what I have seen." He did not write another word.

What he had written before he stopped: genuine beauty is not just pleasing. It has a radiance — splendor — that comes from the thing itself, not from the viewer. It corresponds to something true about reality that goes beyond what paint or stone or light alone can account for.

By the time the user reads "splendor," they have already experienced the concept — they have watched a man spend his life chasing something he could not fully name, and then stop mid-sentence when he finally saw it. The concept lands because the story did the work first.

Curator Note / Description — paste into Sanity
In the basket of fruit at the front of this table, two stray strands of wicker form the outline of a fish. The shadow the basket casts on the tablecloth completes it: a body and a tail. Early Christians used the fish as their sign for Christ, and Caravaggio hid one in the still life before the disciples at the table had found him. The philosophers had a word for what this painting is about: bonum. The Good. Not goodness as a list of rules but the thing every living creature reaches toward. These two disciples walked seven miles beside it before a broken piece of bread opened their eyes.
Hook is the verified ichthys detail — documented by the National Gallery, London. The fish in the wicker and tablecloth shadow is real and specific. From there: bonum named in one sentence, defined by contrast (not rules — the thing every creature reaches toward), then closed by the Emmaus story. Story-first, concept-second. No em dashes.

Tradition Reflection 1 — Augustine — paste into Sanity
Augustine of Hippo (4th–5th century CE)

"Our heart is restless until it rests in Thee."

Augustine of Hippo is one of the most restless minds in the history of Western thought. He spent decades following every version of the Good he could find: philosophy, ambition, pleasure, competing schools of thought. None of it held. The Confessions is the record of a man who chased the transcendental Good across every corner of his life before discovering it had been present all along. He arrived at the same table as the Emmaus disciples, by a much longer road.
Quote: Confessions I.1.1 (c. 401 CE). Verified. "Rests in Thee" is the traditional English rendering. Do not modernize to "You" — the archaic form is part of the text's weight. The parallel to Emmaus (arriving at a table after a long road) closes the reflection without stating it as a lesson.
Tradition Reflection 2 — Aquinas — paste into Sanity
Thomas Aquinas (13th century CE)

"Bonum est diffusivum sui." (The good is self-diffusive.)

Aquinas spent his life building the most systematic account of the Transcendentals the Western tradition has produced. His insight about goodness is simple: it cannot contain itself. Goodness by its nature pours outward, shares itself, communicates. This is not a metaphor. It is the reason fire gives warmth, the reason parents teach their children, the reason a stranger on the road eventually broke bread with two grieving disciples he had just met. The Good does what it is.
Formula attributed to Aquinas drawing on Pseudo-Dionysius. Verified as authentic Thomistic formula. The three examples (fire, parents, stranger) move from physical law to human action to the Emmaus moment — the arc closes inside the reflection itself.
