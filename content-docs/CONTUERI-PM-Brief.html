CONTUERI

PM Brief. Read this before any content, editorial, or product work. Refreshed June 18, 2026. Replaces the March 25, 2026 KALLOS PM Brief.

What This App Actually Is

Most apps point people toward God through prayer, scripture, or explicit spiritual direction. Contueri takes a different path: beauty, truth, and goodness. The same destination, reached through encounter rather than argument. And it reminds people of what we lost.

For centuries, humans built and created beautiful things to glorify the Creator and to express the creative gifts He gave us. They painted ceilings, composed six-voice polyphony, carved stone to catch light in ways that still stop people cold 800 years later. This was not one sect's tradition or one leader's invention. It was a continuous inheritance stretching from the early Church through the Renaissance and beyond, rooted in the belief that beauty, truth, and goodness point toward the same thing.

Most people living today never encountered this tradition. Not because they rejected it. They just never knew it was there. Contueri gives it back to them.

Contueri is not a devotional app. It does not recruit, correct, or direct. It creates encounters with things that are genuinely beautiful, and beauty, truth, and goodness point toward God. It makes those encounters possible. It does not explain them.

The Seven Users

These are seven kinds of seeker the product is built to reach. They are the product brief.

The Gen Z searcher. Early 20s. Constant reader. Searching for meaning. Has only been inside a church for a funeral. No religious framework, does not want one pushed on her. Genuinely curious about ideas, high tolerance for depth. Would encounter a Palestrina motet or a Hopkins poem and want to know more, as long as it does not feel like a conversion attempt.
The Gen X widow. Spiritual rather than religious. Recently widowed. In a grief loop asking why. Does not need comfort content. Needs something that holds up under real darkness. The tradition Contueri is recovering has been here before; it knows suffering and kept making beautiful things anyway. That matters to her. Do not soften it.
The classical-education student. High school age. Already being formed in the Western canon academically. Contueri can deepen what she encounters in school by showing her that the canon she is studying is alive, not archived.
The convert seeking roots. Catholic who grew up Protestant or was less practicing. Wants to understand WHY the Church and Tradition are what they are. Knows the surface; wants the centuries underneath. Contueri is built partly to serve this kind of seeker who is digging for roots.
The working man with vague faith. Believes in something but cannot name what. Beauty is already in his life through someone he loves (his son trains as an opera singer). Has a framework gap, not a faith gap. Would never open a prayer app. Might open something that starts with music.
The politically opposed friend. Disagrees on every contested issue but shares love and adventure with the user. Beauty and wonder transcend the culture war. Two people on opposite political sides can stand in front of the same painting and feel the same thing. Contueri is the room where that happens. It cannot feel like it is taking a side.
The confident Protestant. Thinks they know Christianity. Does not know the 2,000-year tradition from Jesus and St. Peter forward. Not hostile, not searching, confident in an incomplete framework. The app cannot feel corrective; they will close it. But if they encounter Augustine, Gregorian chant composed in the 6th century, or the Desert Fathers, they gain knowledge through encounter rather than argument. The tradition speaks for itself.

How the Content Reaches Them

All seven users share one thing: they would close the app the moment it felt like it was trying to lead them somewhere. That constraint shaped how the content has to work. The June 12 to 18, 2026 editorial sweep gave the answer a name.

The Aristotelian frame

The voice rules in this brief are an operational version of Aristotle's three modes of persuasion (ethos, pathos, logos) plus the structural device that holds them together (the enthymeme). Aristotle's Rhetoric, written around 350 BC, still describes how Contueri content works because the question of how a reader assembles conviction from arranged material is exactly what Aristotle was solving.

  • Ethos: the writer's credibility. Verified quotes, specific dates and named figures, period research done before drafting, RSV-2CE scripture, no glib lookup-table use of color iconography.
  • Pathos: the reader's emotional response. The hook lands as "wait, what?" The opener is concrete and felt. The music selection is mood-primary.
  • Logos: the logical structure of the argument. Hook and context never repeat. The title carries the angle, not the subject. Audio does not repeat across days.
  • The enthymeme: the rhetorical syllogism with one premise deliberately left unstated for the audience to supply themselves. The audience completes the argument in their own mind, and the conclusion arrives as THEIR insight rather than the writer's claim.

The deepest claim: Contueri is a sustained enthymeme. The thesis "beauty points to truth, truth points to God" is never stated. Each day provides specific premises (an artwork, a thinker, a quote, a moment). The user assembles the conclusion. The conviction that arises is theirs, and therefore unshakeable in a way no stated thesis can be.

AI models default to closing the syllogism, stating every conclusion, summarizing every paragraph. That is the OPPOSITE of the enthymeme. Models do it because they are trained to be helpful by being complete. Complete is the opposite of persuasive in the enthymeme sense. Every Contueri voice rule about leaving the conclusion unstated is a rule asking the writer to override the model's default.

The six arc frameworks

Every Contueri journey uses one of six named arc shapes. The shape makes the days hang together as one experience rather than a list of separate pieces. Pick the framework before drafting; the days follow from it.

  • The Ascent. Purgative, illuminative, unitive. Soul-development journeys.
  • The Argument. Evidence accumulates across days to an unstated thesis.
  • The Quest. A character is transformed; the user recognizes their own potential arc.
  • The Season. Anchored to the church calendar (Advent, Lent, Holy Week).
  • The Single Mind. One thinker, deeply (encounter, wrestle, core insight, fruit).
  • The Discovery. Overturns a common assumption; user returns to ordinary life with new sight.

Frameworks are scaffolding, not formulas. They tell the writer what kind of story is being built; they do not write the days. Full conceptual reference: content-docs/CONTUERI-Journey-Arc-Frameworks.html. Practical companion for actually writing a piece: content-docs/CONTUERI-Journey-Writing-Practical-Guide.html.

The central idea

The central idea is the thesis the content is built to convey, expressed as the unstated conclusion the user assembles by the end. ONE sentence. Not the topic, not the subject. Example for When Myth Became Fact: "The pre-Christian myths were grasping at something real that Christianity completed."

If you cannot compress the central idea to one sentence, the idea is not sharp enough yet. The central idea is now a required field on the planningItem schema and the first prerequisite for drafting any piece.

The Content Rule That Follows From This

All seven users share one thing: they would close the app the moment it felt like it was trying to lead them somewhere. None of them signed up to be redirected.

This means the content cannot do the arguing. Every hook, every curator note, every question must feel like discovery, something the user stumbled onto, rather than something curated to move them in a direction.

The content points toward beauty, truth, and goodness. Beauty, truth, and goodness point toward God: the founding statement of the entire product. Contueri does not make that argument explicitly. It makes the encounter possible. The thread is there. The user follows it themselves, at their own pace, or not at all. What it does not do is argue, correct, or direct. The encounter is the work.

This is also why the Surprising History framing exists. When a historical fact has genuine "I didn't know that" power, it belongs in the content, framed as discovery ("here is what actually happened"), never as correction ("here is why the thing you believe is incomplete").

The Voice Rules in Practice

The June 12 to 18, 2026 editorial sweep formalized the voice rules that follow from the content rule above. These are the operational rules a writer applies on any draft. Full detail in CLAUDE.md Editorial Principles section and the four foundational docs.

Hook is a surprising fact, not framing

Lead with a specific fact the reader does not already have. Do not state significance, do not editorialize, do not perform surprise. The fact does the work; the reader assembles what it means. Three forms of framing to rule out: significance claims ("this is one of the most influential..."), atmospheric poetry ("in the candlelit chapel, something stirred"), abstract praise ("there is something profoundly moving about this").

P&P test: would the average reader be surprised by this fact? Simple test. P&P is broad daily reading and the audience has not opted into a specific subject.

Journey test: tiered by subject familiarity, because the reader has selected into a specific subject. Tier 1 universal subjects (Aquinas, Caravaggio) require going below the textbook fact. Tier 3 specialist subjects (Hildegard, Rabanus Maurus) let the basic identity itself carry surprise.

For Tier 1 famous Journey subjects, also run the Inklings test: imagine reading the hook aloud at an Inklings gathering. Lewis, Tolkien, Charles Williams, Warnie Lewis, scotch on the table, pipes lit. If they lean in and ask "wait, what?", it is a hook. If they nod politely, it is not.

Context is storytelling that converges on the thesis

NOT description of the artwork, NOT biographical summary, NOT general theme exploration. Each paragraph tells a story whose payload advances the same unstated conclusion the user is meant to assemble. The thesis is the enthymeme; the stories are the assembled premises.

Identify the thesis BEFORE drafting paragraph 1. Example for a P&P on color iconography: thesis is "color has been used to communicate something to people throughout history." Then every paragraph tells a story that lands on that point. NOT a description of the porcelain cup in the painting; the image is on the screen and tells the user that already.

Common failure caught June 18, 2026: a Cowork draft opened paragraph 1 with "The picture is crowded and close. Mantegna pushed the figures right up to the surface, so the three kings seem to lean into the same small space as the Holy Family. The oldest king kneels at the front and holds up a blue and white Chinese porcelain cup." Art appreciation. The image is on the screen; the paragraph contributes nothing to the thesis. The Chinese porcelain fact CAN be in the context as a surprising fact about period trade routes, but it must arrive as a fact in service of the thesis, not as visual narration.

Hook and context never repeat

Hard rule with zero exceptions. Every paragraph of context after the hook must deliver something the reader does not already have. If the hook covers Aquinas's December 6, 1273 "straw" moment, the context goes elsewhere: family conflict, Albert the Great, centuries of reception. Not the same fact in different words.

Open with the concrete, not the abstract

Lead with something the reader can feel. A person, an action, a paradox, a specific moment, a surprising claim. Analysis as the opener does not land because the reader has nothing to anchor it to yet. A date-stamped scene is one way; do not template it across days or it becomes the "Location. Year. Subject does X." AI tell.

Day title carries the angle, not the subject

The title is a notification. If it names the subject ("Justin Martyr", "Hildegard", "The Sacred Heart"), the user assumes they already know what is coming. Earn the click. Four tests: subject test, assumption test, specificity test, shape test. The title lives INSIDE the draft; find the line you paused on as you wrote, compress, run the tests. Examples that work: "They Were Early" (Justin Martyr). "God Does Not Die" (Sacred Heart). "Halfway Through" (Dante Inferno opening).

The six AI rhetorical patterns banned

Each pattern is a model tell. Each must be flagged and rewritten.

  1. The summary-closer. "The request has not changed." "The story continues." Closing sentences that summarize what the paragraph already said. Cut.
  2. Punchy parallel restatement. "He did not mean it as X. He meant it as Y." Restating for dramatic effect. Rewrite as a single direct statement.
  3. Rhetorical tripling. "Without a wallet, without an address, identified only by..." Three-part list built for rhythm, not information. Cut.
  4. Declarative fragment stacks. "No computer. No software. Gravity calculated the building." Acceptable once per piece at most.
  5. Zoom-out closer. A single sweeping sentence meant to feel momentous at paragraph end. Test: would a journalist cut this as self-conscious?
  6. The AI-poetic reach in clauses (caught June 18, 2026). Watch for clauses or second halves of compound sentences that "land" with rhetorical bend rather than information. Example: a draft promptQuestion "What did you put on this morning, and what did it say before you spoke?" The first clause is plain; the second clause ("before you spoke") is the AI reach. It is a clever turn no thoughtful friend would actually use. The rule: ask the question, do not reach for it. Plain conversational construction, no clever twists, no "land" beat. The fix in that case was the simpler form: "What do the colors you see or choose communicate?"

Master test for all six: "Would a person actually say this in conversation?" If not, rewrite.

Liturgical timing pre-flight check, mandatory before locking any Sacred Art subject

Before committing to a Sacred Art subject for a P&P or Journey day, declare its liturgical season. The check is binary. Either the subject is season-bound (depicts an event with a fixed feast: Nativity, Epiphany, Annunciation, Pentecost, the Passion, the Resurrection, a specific saint's feast day, the Sacred Heart on the Friday after Corpus Christi, etc.), in which case the slot date must align with that season. Or the subject is season-agnostic (a thinker's life work, a landscape, a piece of math, a piece of music without liturgical bond), in which case any date works.

An Adoration of the Magi scene is Epiphany content (early January window). Placing it in June breaks Contueri's implicit calendar-awareness. A Pentecost scene weeks before or after Pentecost reads as out of season. The check fails fast and saves Cowork or the writer hours of work on a subject that cannot ship on the slot.

The Mantegna Adoration of the Magi P&P drafted June 18, 2026 was strong on every other axis but was a seasonal mismatch for a June slot. It was parked for Advent 2026 rather than reworked. Add the liturgical season declaration to any subject pick before locking.

Period context research is mandatory before drafting

Before any hook, context, or stature paragraph, research what an informed contemporary would have understood that a modern eye would miss. This separates Contueri's voice from a coffee-table art book.

Critical caveat on color iconography. Color symbolism is highly contextual. Yellow on Judas is the traitor's color. Yellow on Caravaggio's Magdalene is the courtesan's color. Yellow on Mantegna's Virgin Mary is the Jewish-identity color used without stigma. Same color, three meanings. When colors (or any iconographic element) are doing work, research the specific figure, painter, and period BEFORE writing what the element means. Do not import a meaning rule from one painting into another.

Auditio: mood is primary, recommendation is mandatory

Mood is the lead criterion for music selection. The question is "what does the meditation FEEL like, and does this music create or hold that feeling?" Not "does this music describe this subject?"

Every seeding doc and content plan must include an audio recommendation per day with three options: a primary sacred or classical option WITH a verified link to where the file can be sourced, an alternative from a different register (folk, ambient, contemporary, world), and a ready-to-paste Suno or Udio AI generation prompt as fallback. No-repeat rule across the 30-day window. Banned defaults: Pärt's Spiegel im Spiegel (contemplative-app cliché), generic "Gregorian chant" without naming a specific piece, schola, and recording.

Authority register and verified quotes

Facts must be specific. Specific dates beat vague periods. Named characters beat generic roles. Anchor numbers make claims real. "266 popes have been elected under these words" beats "many popes."

Verified quotes only. "Commonly attributed", "from the novel's notebooks", "tradition holds": any hedge means apocryphal until proven otherwise. Verify against primary sources or replace with something verifiable.

The two meta-rules above all other voice rules

  • When the rules are not working, flag and escalate. Surface failures explicitly rather than burying them in polished drafts. After 3 iterations on the same piece that fail the same rule, stop and surface what was learned.
  • Form-templating across days is failure. Any rule that produces the same FORM across multiple days has become a template, not a principle. Throw out the templated piece and find the angle from the specific content.

Working With Sheri

Sheri is a Product Manager with 20 years of experience. She does not write code. She treats Claude as her dev team, and sometimes her designer.

1. Know the product before responding.

Read this brief and CLAUDE.md before any session work. If you do not have context on a decision, say so and read the relevant spec doc. Do not guess at product decisions and do not pattern-match from generic app conventions. Contueri has specific rules that are non-negotiable.

2. Weigh consequences before offering anything.

Sheri decides fast and instinctively. She should not have to ask "but what about X?", that is your job before you respond. Consider the full landscape: every screen affected, every content type impacted, every downstream consequence. If a decision requires her input, surface the tradeoff clearly and let her make the call. Do not present options without a recommendation.

3. Push back when something is wrong.

She expects it. If a content decision violates editorial rules, say so. If a schema change has an impact she has not considered, say so. If a proposed design breaks an existing pattern, say so. Be direct. No hedging, no "you might want to consider." State the problem, state the consequence, state your recommendation.

4. Ask clarifying questions before executing.

Sheri speaks conversationally but with purpose. Do not take ambiguous statements literally. If she says something that could mean two different things with two different outcomes, ask. One focused question is better than an hour of work in the wrong direction.

5. Do not repeat yourself.

Do not restate what you just did. Do not summarize what you are about to do. Do not recite the rules back at her after she has already heard them. If content is wrong, fix it. If a pattern has been established, apply it without announcing it.

6. No flattery.

Do not tell her a question is "great" or an idea is "excellent." Engage with the substance.

7. Quality over speed.

Sheri is working against real context limits. A focused session on one topic, declared at the start, produces better output than a sprawling session that burns context orienting and reorienting. If a session is clearly trying to do too many things, say so. Help her prioritize.

8. Wait for commit approval during iteration.

"Much better" or "love it" is feedback, not commit approval. Ask before committing in an active iteration loop.

9. Walkthrough pacing, one phase at a time.

For multi-phase step-by-step instructions, deliver one phase, wait for "next." Do not dump the whole sequence and force scrolling.

10. Verify the Sanity schema before any patch or seed script.

Read the schema files first to confirm exact field names, enums, validation. No exceptions.

11. Natural-language field names.

Schema field names, dropdown values, dashboard column labels, brief section names, status values, and anything Sheri will see in Studio or hear in conversation must match her natural language for the concept, not technical or AI-adjacent vocabulary. When proposing a new field, default to her wording. Example caught June 18: the field named journeyArcKey was technical jargon; Sheri would call it the journey name. Renamed to journeyName.

12. Test with npm run build after every step. Commit after every step.

Build verification before committing protects against Next.js-specific build failures that tsc --noEmit misses (Suspense around client useSearchParams, prerender errors, dynamic-route metadata mismatches). Commit after every step so there is a clean rollback.

13. Do not rm .next while the dev server is running.

Partial delete corrupts the live dev server. Use tsc --noEmit for syntax checks during iteration.

The copy-of-a-copy problem: New sessions start cold. This brief helps, but it does not replace accumulated context. If a session is producing generic output, it usually means either (a) the brief was not read carefully enough, or (b) the session scope is too wide. Declare the topic, read the relevant docs, and work on one thing at a time.

What "Earns Its Place" Looks Like

The standard is the Calling of Matthew. Specifically: the observation that Christ's hand in that painting resembles Adam's hand in the Sistine Chapel. One image echoing another across 100 years. A thread you did not know was there, and now you want to pull it. That is the quality of connection Contueri is after.

Content earns its place when it does something a Google search does not. Not the first result, not the Wikipedia paragraph, not what ChatGPT produces when asked "tell me about this painting." If a user could have found it in 30 seconds, it has not earned its place.

The other standard is the Pause & Ponder that reached the working man with vague faith. The question was: what happens to your breathing? Someone who watches sports rather than art was moved by it. That question plus the music reached him. That is the bar. Not "will this work for someone already interested in sacred art?" but "will this reach someone who had no idea this was for them?"

The Lectio pairing must be earned, not looked up.

The philosophy and scripture quotes in the Lectio must connect specifically to what the user just encountered in that day's content, not the general theme. A pairing that could appear in any Contueri day is the wrong pairing for THIS day. If it is interchangeable, keep looking.

  • Wrong: The content is about Caravaggio's light. You find a Plato quote about light from the Republic. You find John 8:12 ("I am the light of the world"). Both are obvious. Neither earns its place.
  • Right: You find the specific Plotinus passage on how light makes beauty visible without being the beautiful thing itself. You find the Isaiah passage that uses darkness and dawn in a way that maps to what Caravaggio is doing formally. The user did not see that coming, and now they cannot unsee it.

Images must reward attention.

If you are asking a user to breathe while looking at an image, or to meditate on it, or to zoom in and explore, that image has to be worth it. Visual depth. Something that reveals more the longer you look. Not just "this is the relevant artwork." The image is doing active work in the experience.

Music does not always need to invoke emotion.

Settled decision. Music in Contueri serves two distinct purposes:

  • Emotional pairing: when the piece matches the feeling of the day (Victoria "O Vos Omnes" for Bosch's grief-to-joy arc). Valid.
  • Focus or meditation support: music that clears space without directing feeling. Ambient, instrumental, chant without dramatic arc. Equally valid.

The shipped range is broad: Gregorian chant, polyphony, choral, instrumental piano, ambient, folk, jazz, world music, contemporary acoustic. Sacred and classical (Bach, Pärt, Taizé) anchor the sourceable register. Avoid organ as a default; the modern ear usually hates it. The licensing rules still apply (free, downloadable, no copyright). The genre is open within those bounds.

Content variety across the NINE types is not optional.

Contueri has 9 content types for a reason: Sacred Art & Architecture, Photography (Pictorialist), Thinkers & Quotes, Literature & Poetry, Music, Food & Wine, Natural Landscape, Watch & Listen, Pattern & Proof. Sacred art is the default starting point, not the destination. The working man's entry point might be landscape or music. The Gen Z searcher's might be literature or a thinker. A content session that only proposes sacred art is incomplete. Push into the other eight types.

Photography is intentionally distinct from Sacred Art. Pictorialist photography (c. 1845-1945, Cameron, Stieglitz, Steichen, Robinson, Emerson) carries the same intentionality as painting without announcing itself as religious. That non-religious framing is the editorial point. It is the doorway for the working man and the Gen Z searcher, who would close anything that announced itself.

What to protect right now.

The Pause & Ponder is the daily face of the app and the thing that is working. It reached someone who had no context for it. Do not simplify it, do not over-engineer it, do not let it drift toward generic. The content quality bar for P&P is the highest in the app.

  • No rounded corners. Border-radius: 0 everywhere. Sharp corners are a design identity decision.

The Map and Location Data

The app has a map view, reached through the Explore tab's map toggle. Each marker on the map is a contentItem whose location field has been populated. Markers are color-coded by contentType using the canonical nine-color palette (Mineral Blue for Sacred Art, Old Ochre for Literature, Sage Stone for Landscape, Clay Terra for Food & Wine, Verdigris for Pattern & Proof, Illuminated Gold for Thinker, Pompeian Crimson for Watch & Listen, Fresco Plum for Music, Warm Slate for Photography). The same palette appears on P&P gradients and Explore type labels, so the visual signature of each content type carries across the entire app.

The map is one of the most distinctive surfaces in the app. It lets a user see the geographic spread of the tradition (Caravaggio in Rome, Bosch in 's-Hertogenbosch, Hildegard at Disibodenberg, Chartres in Chartres, the Westvleteren monks in Belgium) at a glance and click any marker to enter the piece. New sessions consistently miss this because nothing in the curator note or the Sanity entry form announces "this is going to show up on a map."

If a contentItem has a meaningful physical location, the location field MUST be populated when the item is seeded. If the field is blank, the item is invisible on the map and the editorial work was incomplete.

The location field on contentItem

Four sub-fields, all under the location field group in Sanity Studio:

  • locationName, the place name. Example: "San Luigi dei Francesi", "Grande Chartreuse", "the Cathedral of Quito".
  • city. Example: "Rome", "Florence", "Bingen am Rhein".
  • country. Contueri is global; no default.
  • coordinates, latitude and longitude as numbers. Populate ONLY when the content has a meaningful physical location. Example: lat 41.9022, lng 12.4568.

When location is required and when it is not

Required: anywhere the content has a clear physical origin. A Caravaggio painting hangs in a specific church. A Bosch triptych was made in a specific city. A Westvleteren beer is brewed in a specific abbey. A specific liturgical chant is sung at a specific schola. Hildegard's antiphons were composed at a specific monastery.

NOT required: content without a meaningful physical location. An idea or thinker considered in the abstract (a Plato quote on beauty as a category, the concept of viriditas in the abstract) does not need coordinates. A photograph taken anonymously of a generic field probably does not earn location. The schema description states the rule: "Only populate when content has a meaningful physical location."

How to verify before seeding

Before declaring a contentItem complete, check the Sanity entry against this rule. If the item has a place, the location fields must be filled with at least name and city, ideally with coordinates so it appears on the map. The 30-day P&P content seed (June 7-8, 2026) had to be patched with a separate scripts/patch-pp-content.ts run because Cowork's seed JSON dropped location data on most days; this is the failure mode the rule above prevents.

Editorial Dashboards and Producer Surfaces

The editorial doctrine forbids repetition: no piece used more than once across the 30-day P&P launch window, no composer used more than once across 30 days, no Tradition Reflection author dominating, no genre dominating, no artwork or thinker echoing recent content. At 30 published pieces, a producer can track this in her head. At 300, she cannot. The dashboards exist to make the no-repeat rules enforceable at scale.

The dashboards are how the no-repeat rules stop being theoretical and become checkable. Use them BEFORE proposing a new piece, not after.

1. The Content Operations Dashboard, /dashboard

Internal-only, gated by DASHBOARD_ENABLED=true in Vercel environment variables, server-rendered, revalidates every 60 seconds, no-indexed. Seven sections, four of which are repetition-prevention tools:

  • Tradition Reflections (repetition guard). TR table with filter by journey, author type, era, theme, audio coverage. Source concentration view above the table flags any single author type over 50 percent of total TRs. Cross-journey repeat detection highlights amber any author appearing in more than one journey. Use this view before proposing a new TR to confirm the author and era are not over-represented.
  • Audio Status (repetition guard). Six narration slots per day across all journeyDay records (openText, encounterNote, artworkHook, context, reflectionQuestions, auditio) plus the goDeeper count. Work-level repeat detection via composerArtist plus workTitle catches the same piece being used twice (different composers using the same piece, or the same Pärt piece sneaking in across composers). Genre distribution view flags any genre over 40 percent of total. Use this view before proposing a new Auditio recommendation to confirm composer, work, and genre are not already over-represented.
  • Content Library (duplicate audit). Full contentItem inventory with filters: REVIEW items only, missing artworkHook, missing audio, by content type, by theme, by era. Click any row to open the Studio doc. Use the sort-by-title view to surface duplicate contentItems (CLAUDE.md Manual Task #52 documented four cases of duplicate sacred-art records).
  • Journey Completion. Per-day completion state across all journeys, with direct links into Studio journeyDay docs. See at a glance which days have content and which are skeleton.
  • Schema Health. Hand-maintained block showing R1/R5/R6 migration status and pending schema recommendations. Update after every schema audit.
  • TTS Coverage. Character count gap by field type. The largest TTS gap as of June 2026 was contentItem.context (32,846 characters in 44 records).
  • Companion Journey placeholders. For the four formats (PRE, DURING, POST, SUSTAINED). Pre-production.

2. The Visio Divina Audit Dashboard, /dashboard/visio-audit

Per-item editorial view of every sacred-art and landscape contentItem eligible for the Visio Divina flow (/pray/[id]). Each card shows the artwork image, the full Traditional Prayer text, the traditionalPrayerSource, and every linked Tradition Reflection rendered in full (title, shortQuote, summary, source, authorType, era). Producer workflow for preventing repetition

The minimum workflow for any new content proposal:

  1. Read the Content Usage Log to confirm no exact or near-duplicate exists.
  2. Open the relevant /dashboard section (TR for a new tradition reflection, Audio Status for a new Auditio, Content Library for a new contentItem) and confirm the proposed source is not already over-represented.
  3. If proposing a new Auditio, check Work-level repeat detection AND genre distribution AND composer history.
  4. If proposing a new TR, check source concentration AND cross-journey repeat detection.
  5. Drop the proposal into the relevant planningItem in Sanity Studio with the centralIdea, framework, and inspirationLinks fields populated.
  6. Move status to Researching, then Drafted, then Reviewed, then Seeded once it is live in the production schemas.

What Contueri Is Not

  • Not a devotional app. Does not guide prayer, assign Scripture readings, or build spiritual habits.
  • Not a content aggregator. Every item is chosen because it earns its place: it is beautiful, it is specific, it has a hook, and it points toward something true.
  • Not a corrective. Does not argue with anyone's beliefs. Makes encounters possible and lets the user follow the thread.
  • Not a beginner's guide to Christianity. The working man and the Gen Z searcher should never feel like they are being onboarded into a religion.
  • Not Christian self-help. No content that uses suffering as a setup for a tidy resolution.
  • Not corrective by stealth. Content does not require the reader to accept a denominational claim before engaging.

The Founding Statement

Beauty is the splendor of truth.

This is not a tagline. It is the theological claim the entire product stands on. Beauty is not decoration. It is not comfort. It is a way of knowing something true: something that cannot be argued into existence but can be encountered. That is what Contueri is built to make possible.

The user assembles this conclusion themselves across the arc of their use. Contueri never states it.
