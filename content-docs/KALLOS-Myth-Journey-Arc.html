Day by Day -- Provisional Order
Day 1
Arrival
The story that won't stop
Content
Sacred Art
The Great Eleusinian Relief, c. 440 BC, National Archaeological Museum, Athens
Hook
The Eleusinian Mysteries ran for 1,500 years. Every philosopher, general, and emperor of note was initiated. The secret was never written down. Not once. The final revelation, shown in silence to thousands, was a single ear of wheat.
Lectio
"Blessed is he who has seen these Mysteries." (Pindar) / John 12:24 "unless a grain of wheat falls into the earth and dies..."
Music
Phos Hilaron, Byzantine chant (Archive.org) / lyre-harp option: harumachimusic-selene (downloaded)
Question
What story do you keep coming back to that moves you in ways you can't explain?
Day 2
Arrival
The tears she cried
Content
Sacred Art
Francesco Furini, Venus Mourning the Death of Adonis (c. 1626–28, Museum of Fine Arts Budapest, Inv. #493. Oil on canvas, 233 × 190 cm.)
Hook
Furini painted this at 23, already on his way to becoming a Catholic priest. Art historians trace the composition to Raphael's Entombment and Caravaggio's Entombment. But there is a third work pointing to a bigger story: Michelangelo's Pietà.
Lectio
"All things change; nothing perishes." (Ovid, Metamorphoses XV) / Matthew 2:11 — the Magi's gifts including myrrh
Music
Primary: Purcell, Dido's Lament — Dido and Aeneas (1689), Musopen/Archive.org, ~4 min. Alt: Barber, Adagio for Strings (1936), Archive.org, ~8 min.
Question
What is the most beautiful thing you have lost?
Day 3
Arrival
The longing no one could name
Content
Thinker
Plato, via Raphael's School of Athens (1509-11, Vatican)
Hook
Raphael and Michelangelo worked in the same Vatican building, same months, same patron. Neither saw the other's work until both were finished. One room for human reason. One for divine revelation. Raphael gave Plato the face of Leonardo da Vinci.
Lectio
"Beauty itself is the most divine of all things." (Plato) / 1 Cor 13:12 "now we see in a mirror dimly, but then face to face"
Music
Bach, Goldberg Variations Aria (Musopen) / Satie, Gymnopedie No. 1
Question
What do you find yourself reaching for that you cannot quite name?

Addison's Walk, Magdalen College, Oxford
Hook
Sept 19, 1931. Lewis, Tolkien, and Hugo Dyson walked here after midnight in the rain for three hours. Nine days later, Lewis wrote: "I have just passed on from believing in God to definitely believing in Christ."
Lectio
"There are tears at the heart of things." (Virgil) / Ecclesiastes 3:11 "He has set eternity in the human heart"

Day 6
They Saw Dimly
They saw dimly
Content
Sacred Art
Michelangelo, Delphic Sibyl (1509, Sistine Chapel) + Signorelli, Sibyl and David (1499-1502, Orvieto Cathedral)
Hook
Michelangelo put five pagan women on the ceiling of the Pope's chapel, at the same scale as Isaiah and Jeremiah. Not tolerated. Honored. One-third of the prophetic figures on the Sistine ceiling are pagan.
Lectio
"Now a new generation descends from heaven on high." (Virgil, Fourth Eclogue, c.40 BC) / Luke 2:30-32, Nunc Dimittis: "a light for revelation to the Gentiles"

Day 7
They Saw Dimly
As far as he could go
Content
Literature
Dante, Purgatorio Canto XXX (c.1314). The moment Virgil disappears. Dore engraving (1861-68) as visual anchor.
Hook
Dante spent 20 years writing the Divine Comedy. He chose a pagan poet to guide him through the afterlife. In Canto XXX, at the top of Mount Purgatory, Dante turns to tell Virgil what he sees. Virgil is gone. He says Virgil's name three times. He doesn't get to say goodbye.
Lectio
"The hour of departure has arrived." (Socrates, Plato's Apology) / John 14:2-3 "I go to prepare a place for you... I will come again"
Music
Monteverdi, Possente spirto from L'Orfeo Act III (Archive.org) / Vaughan Williams, The Lark Ascending
Question
Have you ever had a guide who brought you to the edge of something they could not follow you into?
Day 8
Invitation
The empty seat
Content
Sacred Art
Andrei Rublev, The Trinity (c. 1425, Tretyakov Gallery, Moscow)
Hook
Rublev painted three figures at a table and left the fourth side open, facing the viewer. The three figures lean toward each other and their postures form a circle around the cup. The cup holds a lamb. The empty seat has been there for 600 years.
Lectio
"Love is the desire for the perpetual possession of the good." (Plato, Symposium) / Revelation 3:20 "Behold, I stand at the door and knock... I will come in and eat with him"
Music
Rachmaninoff, Vespers Op. 37 No. 2 (Archive.org -- Russian Orthodox tradition, same as Rublev) / Arvo Part, Spiegel im Spiegel
Questions
1. Name the question that has been forming in you this week. 2. The seat is empty. What would it take for you to sit down?
What the user feels, day by day
Day 1
Surprise. A secret kept for 1,500 years. A single ear of grain. Something you had filed under "ancient superstition" begins to look like a signal pointing at something real.
Day 2
Grief and beauty. A woman turned into a tree. Her tears become burial spice for a baby. The dying-and-rising pattern is no longer abstract. It has a mother and a body.
Day 3
Recognition. Plato had the same longing you do. He spent forty years pointing toward something he could not name. Neither can you. But the pointing feels familiar.
Day 4
Wonder. A specific path. A specific night. A specific argument that changed a specific man. The stakes stop feeling theoretical.
Day 5
Intellectual pull. Early Christians did not hide from the myth. They painted it on their burial chamber walls. Someone chose to say: this is who Orpheus was always pointing toward.
Day 6
Awe. The Church did not apologize for the pagan prophetesses. It put them on the most famous ceiling in the world. The women who saw dimly are painted in the same room where the Pope prays.
Day 7
Reckoning. The greatest guide in Western literature disappears without a word. The pagan world brought the pilgrim as far as it could. And then it stepped aside.
Day 8
Open. Not a conclusion. A table. An empty seat. The journey ends without closing. The question that has been forming all week lands on the user's doorstep: will you sit down?

The thread that runs through all 8 days

Day 1 opens in a Greek sanctuary with a secret that ran for 1,500 years and died without being written down. Its symbol was a grain of wheat. Day 8 closes in a Russian monastery with an icon that has been inviting someone to sit down for 600 years. The seat is still empty.

In between: a mother whose tears became burial spice for a baby born to die. A philosopher who pointed toward the light his whole life without being able to name it. A rainy walk where the argument was finally made. A burial fresco that said the myth and the event were the same thing. Five pagan women on the ceiling of the Pope's chapel. A poet who led the pilgrim as far as beauty and reason could carry him, and then stepped aside without a word.

The arc is not an argument. It is a following. Every day the user takes one more step in the direction C.S. Lewis walked for 33 years. The journey does not close. It opens into a question the user has to answer for themselves.
