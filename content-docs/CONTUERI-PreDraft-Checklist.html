Pre-Draft Checklist

Load before writing any P&P or Journey content. The two rules at the top prevent most rework. The list below is the self-score before sending a draft.

Do this first, every time, including revisions:
  1. Angle menu before prose. Surface three to five lesser-known, sourced, tier-flagged facts or stories, with a candidate hook for each. Stop for Sheri's pick. Write no prose until the angle is locked. This is project rule 5, and it is the single biggest time-saver. If a piece is not landing, re-open the angle menu, do not rewrite the prose.
  2. Then write it as a story, not a lecture. Open on a concrete scene or a surprising fact and let the idea emerge. Never open on a definition or concept.

Self-score before sending (the draft must pass all)

  • Show, don't tell. No "this was important," "not a pastime." Prove it with a fact or a scene.
  • Every referent concrete. No "she saw / they did / it changed / she began" without the specific what.
  • Surprising hook, not the textbook fact. If it's the most-told thing about the subject, dig deeper.
  • Plain, not writerly. Cut anything that sounds composed ("a meaning it had never carried," "and then it lasted," ornate cataloguing). If it sounds like AI, cut it.
  • Short sentences. Break long ones; the copy is read aloud by TTS.
  • Title and closer withhold and mean something. A title that gives its whole idea away earns no tap. A closer that is only drama says nothing. Model closers on the published days.
  • Only verified facts, sourced to institutions. No smuggled judgments ("late," "funny," "a woman of means"). Source as you write. Flag tiers; present all, don't pre-filter.
  • Every borrowed asset cleared for commercial use first. Audio, translation, image. No non-commercial or personal-use licenses. Verify before proposing.
  • Hook and context never repeat. Each field has one job.
  • Small craft. Describe unknown figures by role, name only the recognizable. Ground an unfamiliar place or term before leaning on it. Give dramatic beats room; don't gloss them. Cut redundant words. No em dashes.

The one-sentence test: my first drafts tend to tell, explain, and reach for the familiar fact in polished prose. The brand wants show, concrete, surprising, plain. Score the draft against that before sending, and items above mostly fix themselves.
