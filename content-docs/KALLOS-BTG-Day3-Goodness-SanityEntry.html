context
The ancient Greeks called them the Transcendentals: Beauty, Truth, and Goodness. Not three separate ideas but three faces of the same reality. Day one: something stopped you and asked you to look. That was beauty. Day two: you followed beauty into meaning and found it corresponds to something real. That was truth. Day three is the arrival. Goodness is what truth calls you toward. Aristotle said every art, every inquiry, every action aims at some good. All of it. Thomas Aquinas gave it a formula: bonum est diffusivum sui. The good is self-diffusive; by nature it pours itself out. That is why the stranger on the road could not stay hidden. Goodness had already announced itself in the basket of fruit. It was waiting for attention to catch up. Caravaggio was 29 when he painted this, living a precarious life in Rome. He used working-class Romans as his models, with weathered faces and worn coats. His patrons were scandalized: saints were supposed to look like saints. He understood something they did not.
First 2 sentences visible; remainder behind "Read more." This is the content item context field -- the collapsible text on the Encounter step. Three paragraphs: (1) Transcendentals three-day arc, closes with the arrival; (2) Aristotle + Aquinas merged -- bonum est diffusivum sui; (3) Caravaggio biography.

Step 2 -- Encounter
curatorNote
In the basket of fruit at the front of this table, two stray strands of wicker form the outline of a fish. The shadow the basket casts on the tablecloth completes it: a body and a tail. Early Christians used the fish as their sign for Christ, and Caravaggio hid one in the still life before the disciples at the table had found him. The philosophers had a word for what this painting is about: bonum. The Good. Not goodness as a list of rules but the thing every living creature reaches toward. These two disciples walked seven miles beside it before a broken piece of bread opened their eyes.
Hook: the verified ichthys in the fruit basket wicker and tablecloth shadow (documented by the National Gallery). Then: introduce bonum as the concept this day names. Five sentences. The hook and the concept meet in the same still life: the answer was present before recognition came. No em dashes.
encounterNote
The innkeeper standing behind them has served dinner to a man crucified three days ago and is already thinking about the next table. The two disciples are about to overturn their chairs. Caravaggio put all three in the same frame: recognition, astonishment, and ordinary life continuing as if nothing changed. The question he left unanswered is which figure you are.
Collapsible "Look Closer" text. Builds the case that failed recognition is not a character flaw but a failure of attention -- which is the philosophical problem this day addresses. All details verified from the painting.
Step 4 -- Reflect
reflectQuestions
What have you been reaching toward lately without being able to name it? What would you call it now?
After leaving the table the disciples asked each other: did not our hearts burn within us while he talked with us on the road? Where has your heart been burning lately, and have you paid attention to it?
Q1 is the Aristotle question made personal: you have been aiming at some good. Name it. Q2 is taken directly from Luke 24:32 and asks the user to find their own Emmaus moment. Both are personal and specific, not hypothetical.

Step 5 -- Go Deeper (Tradition Reflections)
Day 3 is the last day of the 3-day BTG intro journey. Go Deeper appears before Connect. All three reflections below are written to serve the philosophical introduction of Goodness as a transcendental, not just the hospitality theme.
Tradition reflection 1
Augustine of Hippo (4th-5th century CE)

"Our heart is restless until it rests in Thee."

Augustine of Hippo is one of the most restless minds in the history of Western thought. He spent decades following every version of the Good he could find: philosophy, ambition, pleasure, competing schools of thought. None of it held. The Confessions is the record of a man who chased the transcendental Good across every corner of his life before discovering it had been present all along. He arrived at the same table as the Emmaus disciples, by a much longer road.
Tradition reflection 2
Thomas Aquinas (13th century CE)

"Bonum est diffusivum sui." (The good is self-diffusive.)

Aquinas spent his life building the most systematic account of the Transcendentals the Western tradition has produced. His insight about goodness is simple: it cannot contain itself. Goodness by its nature pours outward, shares itself, communicates. This is not a metaphor. It is the reason fire gives warmth, the reason parents teach their children, the reason a stranger on the road eventually broke bread with two grieving disciples he had just met. The Good does what it is.
Tradition reflection 3
Dorothy Day (20th century CE)

"I really only love God as much as I love the person I love the least."

A journalist and activist who spent decades running houses of hospitality for the homeless in New York City. She understood that the Transcendentals do not stay in the realm of philosophy. Goodness always lands somewhere specific: in a meal, at a table, across from a particular face. The question is not whether you believe in the Good in the abstract. It is whether you can recognize it in the person in front of you right now, especially the one you did not choose to sit beside.
