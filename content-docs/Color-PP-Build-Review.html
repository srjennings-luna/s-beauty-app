Hook

In 1546 the Duke of Florence, Cosimo I de' Medici, passed a law about a single color. Every prostitute in the city, women who dressed in the silks and jewels of the nobility, was now required to wear a yellow veil in public, so that no man could mistake her for a noblewoman. Artists of the time used the same code.
Context

The painters were working in the same code. Look at almost any old Last Supper and one man is wrapped in yellow. It is Judas, in the color of the coins he took. Yet the same color, beaten into gold, was the light of heaven, the stuff of every halo. One pigment could crown a saint or shame a traitor, depending where it was laid.

Once you notice this, the rest of the picture speaks. The blue on the Virgin's gown was the costliest thing in the workshop. The deep reds came from a guarded Spanish secret that turned out to be a dried insect, and still tints the lipstick and strawberry yogurt on your shelf.

By now the apple on the ledge is not a still life. The cucumber beside it was read as the cure that was promised. The peacock stands for the flesh that never rots. Even the caged bird carries the Passion.

The painters worked in the same code, and people read it without effort. A color was not decoration. It was information.

So when a painter wanted to single out the man who would sell the rest, he reached for yellow. The traitor wears it in picture after picture.

And here the color does something strange. That same yellow, laid down as gold, was the most sacred substance a painter owned. Where he set it decided whether it shamed a man or crowned him.

Look what this painter did with it. He ran a thread of gold down a small-town street, through a hole in a wall, into the room of a young woman kneeling alone. It does not fall on a prince. It falls on her.

The color a city made its outcasts wear, and the color a painter used for the light of God, were the same color. It only ever depended on where it fell.

People read it without effort. The color of a figure's garment told you whether he could be trusted. When a painter wanted to mark a man, he reached for yellow. In Giotto's fresco of the betrayal, in the chapel at Padua, Judas leans in for the kiss wearing a yellow cloak. The color names the traitor.

The same language could mark a woman, and on Mary Magdalene it marked something that was never true. For centuries painters dressed her as the penitent sinner, and the color they reached for was red. We read that red now as love, or devotion. Her own century read it as the opposite. A scarlet cloak and loose, uncovered hair were the period's sign of a woman who had, in the language of the day, given herself to every delight of the body. The Gospels say none of it. She belonged to the circle of women who provided for Christ and his disciples. She stayed at the cross when the others had run, and she was first to find the tomb empty. The woman in the Gospels and the woman on the wall had become two different people.

For centuries the verdict held while the color under it changed. The identity itself began as a mix-up. In the sixth century Pope Gregory the Great folded Mary of Magdala together with two other women in the Gospels, and the penitent sinner was born from that mistake and carried for the better part of a thousand years. Red marked her through most of it. Then, as the law made yellow the color of prostitutes, painters reached for yellow too and dressed her in it alongside the red. The sin they were marking never changed. Only the color did. The Church itself untangled the three women in 1969 and changed the readings for her feast, from the sinful woman to her meeting with the risen Christ in the garden. By then the correction lived quietly in documents, but the paintings had been speaking for centuries.

A. The Before column

  • "What did contemporaries understand that the modern eye misses." The engine of the whole piece. The strongest line in the opening request.
  • "Around the time of the Reformation." A period anchor abandoned within two turns. Cost a research pass.
  • "Color iconography as a learned language." The conceptual spine. The most important early steer.
  • "Multiple colors across one canvas." A reasonable instinct that produced the color tour we later dismantled.
  • "CS Lewis style story form." Core voice target, repeated usefully.
  • "Juicy stuff they can relate to." Produced the lipstick-and-yogurt aside that broke the register.
  • "Find a story outside of sacred art." The cochineal dead-end, reversed the next turn.
  • "The reader begins to rethink the language they've missed." Sharpened the thesis to its final form.
  • "Prostitutes wore yellow. Shocking." The hook itself, arriving with verifiable sourcing.
Pattern. Green inputs were about thesis and voice. Red inputs were about coverage and accessibility, and they pulled toward the survey-and-wink draft we had to undo.

B. The After column

  • "Read the full P&P content in Sanity, not the seeding docs." The decisive move. Reading the live pieces fixed more than any description of the voice.
  • Holy Week, eight days. Taught the one-fact reversal hook, cost-as-story, and reading one hidden detail as the climax.
  • When Myth Became Fact, Beauty Truth & Goodness, They Were Early, Gaudi, Sacred Heart. The converging-story form, the quiet enthymeme close, the hook detail living in the painting.
  • One friction. Published hooks use fragment stacks the rules ban, so the corpus and the rules disagree.
Pattern. Naming specific published pieces is faster and more reliable than describing the voice in the abstract.

C. The standing docs I read first summary

Really helpful

  • The enthymeme: leave the conclusion for the reader. The idea that most separates good Contueri copy from generic copy.
  • Hook is a surprising fact, not framing. Context converges on the thesis and never narrates the screen. Hook and context never repeat. The red failure examples taught more than the abstract rules.

Did not land, ambiguous, or contradictory

  • Meta-Rule 3 versus the published form. "Give multiple varied examples" reads as license for a tour, which is what produced the Before column. The breadth should ride one converging story, not a list.
  • The fragment-stack contradiction. Rules ban them; approved copy uses them. Needs a rule of thumb.
  • The voice lives where I was not sent. Add "read two or three current published pieces before drafting" to the standing instructions.
  • Navigability. CLAUDE.md is large and carries many superseded docs. A short "current canon" list at the top would help a cold session.

D. Levers for faster turnaround

  • Point me at exemplars, not adjectives. "Match the hook and context of They Were Early and the Sacred Heart day" sets the voice in one line.
  • Lock the anchor and thesis early. One line: the painting, the thesis, the detail that pays off. The long menus cost the most time.
  • Watch the coverage words. "Cover multiple colors" triggers a survey, which is off-voice. "One story, let the rest stay implied" keeps it Contueri.
  • Lead with the shocking fact. The yellow-veil law did more in one message than three rounds of angle exploration.

E. The Final column what the edits did

The editing phase, dotted the same way.

Moved it toward the final

  • "It begs the Mary Magdalene painting." The pivot from Crivelli to Magdalene, the biggest move of the edit phase. A clever color tour traded for a human story that embodies the thesis.
  • "The spine is what color communicates, not a specific color. The meaning stays, the color changes." Locked the spine and dissolved the red-versus-yellow tangle by making the change itself the point.
  • "I need verified sources, museums, the Church, professors." Raised the piece to the ethos standard and caught weak sourcing before it could ship.
  • The fact-checks, one after another. Woman of means, the prostitute story predating the Dominican, the red-first question. Each forced an accuracy fix, and the red-to-yellow correction became the engine.
  • "Look at the published endings for the finishing style." Fixed the ending by anchoring on proven closers.
  • "Drop the last paragraph." Removed on-screen narration and trusted the reader.
  • The line edits. Documents and quietly, pictures to paintings, "her" for "one," the actio tightened to one beat.
  • "Remove the Orthodox and Protestant call-outs." Protected the tone for a Catholic-primary reader.
  • "Just name Judas." Undid a coy phrasing I had over-engineered.

Detours, or fixes I had to be told to make

  • My first sources were blogs and book-promotion pages. Should have gone to institutions first. The error that cost the most trust.
  • I wrote "a woman of means." The text does not say it. You caught it.
  • I took "you don't need to name Judas" too literally. Over-applied a note until it read as coy.
  • The Crivelli anchor and the yellow-to-gold reversal. A whole branch built and set aside. Useful exploration, not the final.
  • My paragraph-four ending, the Latin reading as narration. Did not work, dropped.
  • The red-light district and the yellow star. Good in conversation, never belonged in the piece.
Levers for next time.
  • Source every factual claim to an institution as it is written, not after.
  • When a rule tempts me to be coy, check whether the story actually wants the plain version.
  • Pivots are cheap early and expensive late. Test "which human story carries this" before building the reading.
