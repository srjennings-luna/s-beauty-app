Revised — paste into Sanity

C.S. Lewis was eight years old when he read a line in a poem about Norse mythology. He could not have told you what the poem meant. He only knew that it made him ache for something he couldn't name.

He spent the next thirty years chasing that feeling. He followed it into music, into landscape, into other people's poetry. Each time it moved him, it also refused to fully satisfy him. Like following a scent you can never trace to its source.

He eventually concluded that this was the point. The longing itself was the message.

You don't have to agree with what he concluded. But you probably know the feeling.

Curator Note / Description (main encounter text)
Current

C.S. Lewis called it "joy" — not happiness, but a kind of longing that comes when beauty touches us. It's the feeling you get when a song unexpectedly moves you to tears. When you turn a corner in a city and time stops. When you read a line someone wrote centuries ago and feel completely understood. This feeling is not sentimental. Revised — paste into Sanity

He called it "Joy" with a capital letter, to distinguish it from happiness. Happiness was contentment. Joy was different: a sudden sharp longing, arriving without warning, pointing toward something it could never provide.

He found it in a toy garden his brother had made from a biscuit tin lid, moss and small stones arranged inside it. He found it in a particular kind of autumn light. He found it in that Norse poem. And he discovered, over decades, that every time he reached for the thing Joy seemed to be pointing at, the Joy vanished — and whatever he'd grabbed wasn't it.

What he eventually understood: Joy was not pointing at any earthly thing. It was pointing past all of them.

Why: Specific details (biscuit tin lid, moss and stones, autumn light) do the work that the abstract description is trying to do. The user experiences the concept rather than reading its definition.

Opening Text
Current

At some point, every genuine beauty asks the same silent question: "Where do I come from? What does it mean that I exist?"

Revised — paste into Sanity

In 1599, Caravaggio painted an altarpiece for the French church in Rome. The church rejected it.

Matthew looked too much like a laborer, they said. His feet were dirty. He didn't look like a saint. Caravaggio, who had already killed a man by this point and was wanted for assault in three separate incidents, painted it again.

In the second version (the one hanging in Rome now), Matthew sits in a dark corner counting money at a tax collector's table. The light comes from outside the frame, from a source you cannot see. It cuts across the room at an impossible angle. A finger points. Matthew looks up, startled, and touches his own chest as if asking: me?

Why: Opens with the specific story of the rejected painting — which carries the same meaning as the abstract thesis question but shows rather than states it. The "me?" is the question beauty asks. No need to say it directly.
Curator Note / Description
Current

Thomas Aquinas, the great medieval theologian, defined truth this simply: it is the correspondence between the mind and reality. The painting is beautiful because it corresponds to something real about calling, about light, about transformation. It's not making something up. It's showing us what is actually true about how love works. And Aquinas understood something crucial: all genuine beauty is a form of truth. When something is truly beautiful, it is because it reveals something real.

Revised — paste into Sanity

Thomas Aquinas was the strangest child his monastery had ever received. His father sent him to Monte Cassino at age five. The monks watched him grow into a boy who barely spoke and appeared to be thinking about one question for years at a time. His classmates called him "the dumb ox."

He became one of the most influential philosophers of the medieval world. In the last seven years of his life, he wrote the Summa Theologiae, a total synthesis of everything he understood about God, beauty, and how they connect. He never finished it. On December 6, 1273, something happened to him during Mass. He set down his pen and said: "All that I have written seems to me like straw compared to what I have seen." He didn't write another word.

What he had written before he stopped: genuine beauty isn't just pleasing. It has a radiance — splendor — that comes from the thing itself, not from the viewer. It reveals something about reality that goes beyond what paint or stone or light alone can account for.

Why: The Aquinas story carries the concept without explaining it. By the time the reader reaches "splendor," they have already experienced the idea — they've watched a man spend his life chasing something he couldn't fully name, and then stop mid-sentence when he finally saw it.

Curator Note / Description — what replaces the moved paragraph
Current Curator Note (keep as new Opening, move it up)

There is a monastery in southern Italy that has made bread for eight centuries... [moved to Opening Text above]

Revised Curator Note — paste into Sanity

The Rule of Saint Benedict, written in 530 AD, includes specific instructions for bread: who bakes it, how it is distributed, that the portion for the sick should be larger. Benedict was not primarily a mystic. He was a man who thought very carefully about bread.

He also wrote: "All guests who present themselves are to be welcomed as Christ." Not as if they were Christ. As Christ. The stranger arriving hungry at the gate is Christ. The bread you put in their hands is what you put in his.

He wrote this while the Roman Empire was collapsing around him. Roads were unsafe. The economy had broken down. He sat down and wrote instructions about bread and hospitality that have been followed, continuously, for fifteen hundred years.

The Context/Look Closer field can stay as is — it has the Benedictine hospitality explanation, which works well as background enrichment.
