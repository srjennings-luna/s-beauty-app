Section 1What KALLOS Is

KALLOS is a daily contemplative discovery app built around beauty, truth, and goodness. It enters through beauty and asks nothing about belief. It is not a prayer app, not a devotional, not a mindfulness app. The audience is the curious seeker, someone moved by beauty who has no framework for what it points toward. Faith background is not a prerequisite; only curiosity is required.

Positioning: KALLOS occupies the space between secular mindfulness (Calm, Headspace) and explicitly religious apps (Hallow, Glorify). Neither category does what KALLOS does. It is the room where the firefighter and the seminarian can stand in front of the same painting and feel the same thing.

Section 2Voice & Tone Rules (Non-Negotiable)

The Dinner-Table Test

Every curator note must open with one surprising, specific, shareable fact, something the user genuinely didn't know and would repeat at dinner. This is not optional. It is the non-negotiable entry point for every encounter. Without a hook, the curator note defaults to atmosphere or significance claims, which give the user nothing to stand on.

The test: Would someone say "wait, I didn't know that" and repeat it at dinner? If yes: hook. If not: keep looking.

Story First, Not Lecture First

Lead with a story, not a lecture or a list of facts. The opening text for any day should read like the beginning of something you want to hear more of. A person, a place, a moment. Human emotion. Something happened, and it mattered. The user leans in because the story is interesting, not because they've been told the content is important.

Think C.S. Lewis: he never told you what to think. He told you a story, or described an experience, and by the time you finished you had arrived at the idea yourself. That is the KALLOS voice. The user should feel like they are discovering something that feels like their own idea, not sitting in a classroom being told what the painting means.

The test: Is this dinner-conversation interesting? Could you tell this to someone over a glass of wine and watch their eyes widen? If it reads like a textbook or a museum placard, rewrite it as a story. Find the human being in the history. Find the moment something changed. Start there.

Every opening, every curator note, every context field should connect to the theme of the journey through narrative, not through explanation. The user does not need to be told "this connects to our theme of X." The connection should be felt, not announced.

Banned

  • No em dashes. They are a telltale sign of AI-generated text. Use a comma, a period, or a short hyphen. Rewrite the sentence if it needs an em dash to hold together.
  • Banned words: "journey" (for the experience itself), "sacred," "profound," "transformative," "spiritual practice," "invitation to...," "encounter with the divine" (generic phrase), "nourish your soul." These words carry no information.
  • No first person (I, my, me). The app does not refer to itself or its curator as a person.
  • No app-speak. Never "Welcome to Day 3!" or "Today we'll explore..." or "Tap to continue." The user is in a contemplative state. Don't break the spell.
  • Never use "shaft" to describe light. Use "ray" or "stream" instead.
  • Never say "non-Catholic" as a user category. The audience is always "the curious seeker."
  • Never use "Surprising History" as a label. Frame historical facts as discovery, never apologetics.

Required

  • Short, weighted sentences. In espresso mode, text appears on a dark screen with cream text. Long paragraphs feel heavy and hard to read.
  • Assume no one knows the content. Not the artist, not the story, not the symbol, not the thinker's name. Write for the brilliant, curious person who has never heard of Hildegard of Bingen or what chiaroscuro means.
  • Explain symbolism inline, every time, in plain language. "The peacock was a standard symbol of immortality; its flesh was believed not to rot."
  • Eucatastrophe must be defined inline on first use: "a eucatastrophe, a sudden joyful turn after the long darkness."
  • Trust the content. The painting will move the user. Describe, don't explain. Frame, don't interpret. Point, don't lecture.
  • Earn the emotional moment. Don't start at the peak. Day 1 should be warm and inviting. Let the drama build through the week.

One Detail Per Field

The hook lives in the Curator Note. Once. Every other field is a different door into the same room. If a detail appears in more than one field, the user reads it twice without knowing they've been here before. The painting has hundreds of objects. Use them.

Field Jobs

Field Job
Brief Description The visual anchor. What it looks like, scale, composition, dominant visual fact.
Curator Note The hook. One surprising dinner-table fact. Lives here and ONLY here.
Context The story behind the piece. History, commission, reception, what the original audience knew.
Opening Text The emotional threshold. Mood, not information. A different specific detail than the hook.
Encounter Note The symbol decoder. Specific objects, visual elements, and what they meant to the original audience.

Section 3The Lectio Pairing Structure

Philosophy first (the question), scripture second (the arrival). Two voices arriving at the same truth from different directions. The firefighter enters through philosophy; the parishioner arrives at scripture through a passage they've never read this way. One structure, two audiences.

Current standard: One-liner pre-Christian philosopher for the philosophy slot. Modern thinkers (Tolkien, Lewis) in the philosophy slot violates this rule and needs updating.

Section 4Journey Structure - 6 Steps

Every Journey day follows the same 6-step structure. The user navigates via Stories-style swipe (thin 2px progress bar at top). All steps render in espresso mode: dark background (#16110d), cream/sage text.

1. OPEN
1-3 sentences. An invitation, not a summary. "The first words someone whispers in a dark room." Never a lesson plan.
2. ENCOUNTER
The main event. The user meets the painting, poem, landscape, or idea. Curator Note (hook) + Encounter Note (symbols) + Context (history).
3. BREATHE
No writing needed. Full-bleed image with 8x pinch-to-zoom and a breathing animation. Choose images with rich detail that reward close looking.
4. REFLECT
1-2 questions connecting the content to the user's own experience. Build across the week's arc. By Day 4 you can reference earlier days.
5. CONNECT
One sentence threading today to tomorrow. Creates curiosity without spoiling. Day 7 uses a closing reflection instead.
6. GO DEEPER
2-3 tradition reflections (Church Fathers, Saints, Popes, theologians). Plain language. Varied eras: one ancient, one medieval, one modern.

Emotional Arc (7-Day)

Days 1-2: Arrival

Curiosity, wonder, surprise. Low threshold. Easy to sit with, no background needed.

Days 3-5: Deepening

The question gets richer. The user starts making connections between days. More complex content.

Days 6-7: Integration

The theme lands. The user sees the week's thread clearly. Day 7 feels like an arrival, not a final exam.

Integrated Curriculum

The question stays the same. The encounter changes. Use at least 5 of 8 content types across 7 days. No two consecutive days same type.

8 Content Types: Sacred Art, Literature, Music, Thinker, Landscape, Math/Science, Food/Wine, Watch/Listen.

Section 5Current Work - When Myth Became Fact

Central Question: What if the oldest human stories were reaching for something real?

A 7-day journey following C.S. Lewis's argument: the universal human impulse to tell stories about dying and rising gods was not wishful thinking, but a signal reaching forward through time. Version 3.1, Sanity-ready. Every field maps to a named Sanity field.

Arc

Days 1-2: The Pattern

Eleusinian Mysteries / Great Eleusinian Relief. Venus/Adonis pending for Day 2.

Days 3-4: The Argument

Plato via Raphael's School of Athens. Addison's Walk, Magdalen College, Oxford (landscape day).

Days 5-6: They Saw Dimly

Sistine Chapel and Orvieto Cathedral sibyls (pagan prophetesses on the Pope's ceiling). Dante's Purgatorio (the pagan guide who goes as far as he can, then stops).

Day 7: The Invitation

Rublev's Trinity icon. The empty seat at the table, facing the viewer for 600 years.

Key Decisions Made

  • Day 1 opens with the Eleusinian Mysteries. Hook: the secret that died with the last initiates in 392 AD. A single ear of wheat, held up in silence.
  • Day 1 Opening Text was rewritten April 9 to tell the full Persephone myth. Replaced the earlier "lecture mode, thesis-first" version.
  • Version A (MythBecameFact, 5-day, ends with "Rabboni"/Fra Angelico) vs Version B (CSLewis, 7-day, ends with Till We Have Faces) were compared. Decision: merge the best of both into v3.
  • Justin Martyr moved from Journey Day 4 to a standalone Pause & Ponder day with CTA to this journey. His hook (walking into the Emperor's court to defend Christianity, later beheaded) and the Heraclitus/John Logos lectio pairing are the strongest in either version.
  • Isenheim Altarpiece replaced with Dante's Purgatorio (better fit for this journey's arc).
  • Rublev's Trinity icon chosen for Day 7 close. The empty seat at the table is the invitation that ends the journey without an argument.
  • Markos "Myth Made Fact" research incorporated (general vs. special revelation framework).
  • Sistine Chapel and Orvieto Cathedral sibyls added for "They Saw Dimly" days. Pagan prophetesses flanked by Hebrew prophets as equals.

Confirmed Lectio Pairings

Day Philosophy Scripture
Day 1 Pindar (Eleusinian Dirges fragment, c. 470 BC) John 12:24 ("unless a grain of wheat falls into the earth and dies")
Day 3 Plato (Form of Goodness or Beauty) 1 Corinthians 13:12 ("now we see in a mirror dimly")
Day 4 Virgil ("There are tears at the heart of things") Ecclesiastes 3:11 ("eternity placed in the human heart")

Hooks That Passed

  • Eleusinian: the lost secret that died with the last initiates. The grain of wheat shown in silence.
  • Plato/Raphael: Leonardo da Vinci's face used for Plato. OR: Raphael and Michelangelo painting in adjacent rooms, same patron, same months, neither seeing the other's work.
  • Addison's Walk: September 19, 1931. Three men walking after midnight in the rain. Nine days later, Lewis writes to Greeves: "I have just passed on from believing in God to definitely believing in Christ."
  • Rublev: the empty fourth seat at the table, facing the viewer, for 600 years.

Section 7Audio Sourcing Rules

  • Must be free to download and use with no copyright restrictions. No Spotify, Apple Music, YouTube-only, or copyrighted recordings.
  • Approved sources (in order): Pixabay Music, Musopen, Free Music Archive, Internet Archive, gregorian-chant-hymns.com, Bandcamp free downloads with explicit free-use license.
  • MP3 preferred. Convert MP4/AAC to MP3 before uploading to Sanity.
  • Match the story, not the period. The music should match the emotional arc and narrative of the day, not necessarily the historical period of the artwork. A Byzantine chant doesn't have to pair with a Byzantine painting.
  • Option A vs Option B must be genuinely different. Different sound, different genre, different mood. Never two similar tracks as options. If Option A is a chant, Option B should be something structurally different.
  • For the Myth journey: Audio across all 7 days should be chosen like a playlist. The full set should feel curated and intentional as a sequence, not just individually appropriate per day.
  • Genre is open, but non-sacred/classical must earn its place.

Section 8Go Deeper Rules

  • Plain language, always. If you need to mention "the Beatific Vision," explain it in the same sentence.
  • Connect to the day's specific content, not just the theme in general. The more specific the connection, the more the user feels the thread.
  • Variety of eras within each day: aim for one ancient/patristic, one medieval, one modern. The user should feel 2,000+ years of thinking.
  • Accuracy matters. Meister Eckhart is not a saint. De Caussade is not a saint. Aquinas is a saint AND a Doctor of the Church. Research before writing.
  • Write as if explaining to a smart friend over coffee. No jargon, no academic apparatus, no assumed knowledge of Church history.

Section 9Hook Guide by Content Type

The hook changes shape depending on what you're working with. In every case, the test is the same: would someone say "I didn't know that" and repeat it at dinner?

Content Type Where to Find the Hook
Sacred Art Exterior panels, original location (above an altar? in a private room?), what was painted over or lost, a hidden detail most reproductions crop out, what the original audience understood that we no longer do.
Literature Circumstances of composition (written in a night, written dying, published after death), a line the author cut, what the title actually means in the original language.
Music The premiere reaction (booed or wept?), a structural choice most listeners never notice, what the piece was originally written for vs. how it's now performed.
Thinker The moment that broke their thinking open, the question they spent a lifetime failing to answer, the conversation that changed everything.
Landscape The geological or ecological fact that changes how you read the place, what the land was used for that most visitors don't know.
Math/Science The discovery that arrived backwards, the number that kept appearing where it shouldn't, the scientist who was laughed at and turned out to be right.
Food/Wine How long the tradition actually is (centuries, not decades), the moment it nearly disappeared, the one step that cannot be hurried.
Watch/Listen What the filmmaker originally intended to make, the moment the argument turns, the reaction no one expected.
