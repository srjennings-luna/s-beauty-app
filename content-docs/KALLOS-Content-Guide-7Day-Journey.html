KALLOS Agent Content Instructions

7-DAY JOURNEY

How to write a journey - the integrated curriculum, the 6-step structure, what every field needs

What a Journey Is

A Journey is a 7-day guided experience that explores one of KALLOS's founding themes through a different content type each day. Each day follows the same 6-step structure - Open, Encounter, Breathe, Reflect, Connect, Go Deeper - creating a rhythm the user settles into. The goal is not to teach the theme. It's to let the user discover it themselves through seven encounters with beauty. By Day 7, the theme should feel less like a concept and more like something they've lived with all week.

The audience is the curious seeker. Every Journey uses the classical education approach: leading users toward beauty, truth, and goodness without announcing that's what's happening. The content never assumes faith. It assumes curiosity. A Journey is not a course, not a devotion, not a reading plan. It is seven days of paying attention to the same question from seven different angles.

The Integrated Curriculum Model

This is the single most important concept for Journey content. Every day features a different content type, but all seven days answer the same central question - the Journey's guiding theme.

The question stays the same. The encounter changes. Day 1 might explore "What does light reveal?" through sacred art. Day 2 explores it through landscape. Day 3 through music. The user doesn't need to be told the connections - the connections emerge naturally because every encounter points to the same truth from a different direction.
Example - Light Journey
Central Question: "What does light reveal that darkness hides?"

Day 1: Sacred Art - Caravaggio's use of chiaroscuro
Day 2: Landscape - The Caves of Lascaux, painting by firelight
Day 3: Literature - Gerard Manley Hopkins, "God's Grandeur"
Day 4: Thinker - St. Hildegard of Bingen on living light
Day 5: Music - Arvo Pärt, "Spiegel im Spiegel"
Day 6: Pattern & Proof - The physics of light and the visible spectrum
Day 7: Watch/Listen - A documentary on stained glass and how cathedrals use light

Content Type Distribution

Each Journey should use at least 5 of the 8 content types across its 7 days. No two consecutive days should feature the same type. Aim to open with a strong visual day (sacred art or landscape) and close with something that pulls the week together (watch/listen, thinker, or literature work well for Day 7).

Emotional Arc Across 7 Days

A Journey is not a flat list. It has a shape. Think of it in three movements:

DaysMovementFeels Like
1–2ArrivalCuriosity, wonder, surprise. The user encounters something beautiful and feels drawn in. Low threshold - easy to sit with, no background needed.
3–5DeepeningThe question gets richer. The user starts making connections between days. You can bring in more complex content - a thinker, a musical piece that requires patience, a landscape that demands stillness. The middle is where transformation happens.
6–7IntegrationThe theme lands. The user sees the week's thread clearly. Day 7 should feel like an arrival, not a final exam - "Oh, that's what this was about." The closing day earns its meaning from everything that came before.

The 6-Step Daily Structure

Every Journey day follows the same 6 steps. The user navigates via Stories-style swipe (thin 2px progress bar at top, swipe left/right, no Continue button). All steps render in espresso mode - dark background (#16110d), cream/sage text.

STEP 1 OPEN

Purpose: Set the day's frame. Create a moment of stillness before the encounter begins.

What's shown: A full-bleed opening image with a brief text overlay (1–3 sentences). The image is always present - it is the visual anchor for the entire day.

Writing guidance: The opening text is not a summary of the day. It's an invitation. It should feel like the first words someone whispers in a dark room. Short. Evocative. It frames without explaining. Think of it as the "once upon a time" - the sentence that makes the user lean in.

Good Opening Text
"You came here because something moved you. A painting in a museum. A line of poetry. A piece of music. Something you couldn't quite name, but it mattered. That feeling is not an accident. It is an invitation."

"The monks of Lascaux had no words for what they were doing. They had firelight, mineral pigment, and a wall that had waited forty thousand years. They painted anyway."
Bad Opening Text
"Today we will explore how Caravaggio used light and shadow to create dramatic tension in his paintings." - a lesson plan, not an invitation

"Welcome to Day 3 of the Light journey! Today's content is a poem by Gerard Manley Hopkins." - app interface copy, not contemplative writing
STEP 2 ENCOUNTER

Purpose: The main event. The user meets the content - the painting, the poem, the landscape, the idea.

What's shown: Content image (no zoom), title, description, collapsible context ("Read more"), quote/scripture, optional audio player, encounter guidance.

Writing guidance: The Encounter step relies on the Content Item fields (same schema as Pause & Ponder). The key difference: Journey encounter content is written to serve the week's arc, not to stand alone. Every description, context field, and curator note should be flavored by the Journey's central question - even if it never names the question directly.

Encounter Guidance is the Journey-specific layer. This is where you can nudge the user toward a particular way of engaging: "Listen with your eyes closed for the first minute." or "Look at the hands first. Then the face." It's optional but powerful - one sentence that changes how the user encounters the content.

STEP 3 BREATHE

Purpose: A dedicated contemplative pause. The user sits with the day's image - no text, no questions, just the image and a breathing animation.

What's shown: Full-bleed image with 8x pinch-to-zoom, a pulsing dot breathing animation (infinite loop), and helper text: "Sit with this image and let your eyes explore."

Writing guidance: You don't write anything for this step. It uses the same opening image from Step 1. The Breathe step is programmatic - the app handles the zoom, animation, and helper text. Your job is to choose an opening image that rewards close looking. An image with rich detail, texture, layered composition, or a sense of depth works best. The user should be able to zoom in and discover things they missed at first glance.

STEP 4 REFLECT

Purpose: Move from encounter to personal reflection. One or two questions that connect the content to the user's own experience.

What's shown: Questions displayed one at a time, large text, centered on screen.

Writing guidance: Reflection questions for a Journey are different from P&P prompt questions. P&P questions are standalone - they work without any other context. Journey questions build on the day's encounter AND the week's arc. By Day 4, you can reference themes from earlier days. By Day 7, the question should feel like a culmination.

Good Journey Reflection Questions
Day 1: "What is the most beautiful thing you've encountered in the past year? What did it make you want to do?"
Day 4: "You've spent three days with light in different forms - paint, earth, words. What pattern are you starting to notice?"
Day 7: "If light reveals what darkness hides - what has this week revealed to you?"
Bad Journey Reflection Questions
"What does this painting teach us about God's presence?" - assumes a framework the user may not hold
"How did you feel about today's content?" - too vague, no entry point
"What are three things you learned this week?" - homework, not reflection
STEP 5 CONNECT

Purpose: Thread the current day to the next. Create anticipation without spoiling.

What's shown: Dark background with the next day's image as a subtle drift animation. One sentence of text.

Writing guidance: The connect thread is one sentence. It should create a question in the user's mind - not answer one. It links today's encounter to tomorrow's by hinting at the shift without revealing what's coming. On Day 7, the connect thread is replaced by a closing reflection - a sentence that sends the user back into their life with the theme still echoing.

Good Connect Threads
Day 1 → 2: "Beauty is never accidental. It always points somewhere. Tomorrow, we meet a painter who understood this perfectly."
Day 3 → 4: "Words can carry light. But what happens when light speaks for itself?"
Day 6 → 7: "You've followed light through paint, stone, sound, and science. Tomorrow - one last encounter. And a question you've been circling all week."
Bad Connect Threads
"Tomorrow we'll look at a poem by Hopkins." - spoils the encounter, no curiosity
"Continue your journey tomorrow!" - generic app copy
STEP 6 GO DEEPER

Purpose: Optional tradition reflections for users who want to explore the intellectual and spiritual roots of what they just encountered.

What's shown: Expandable bars. Each bar shows a voice from the tradition - a Church Father, Saint, Pope, theologian, mystic, or Doctor of the Church - with a short quote and a plain-language summary.

Writing guidance: See the "Go Deeper / Tradition Reflections" section below for full field specs and voice guidance. Typically 2–3 reflections per day. Each should connect the tradition voice to the day's specific content, not just the theme in general.

Journey-Level Fields

These fields live on the Journey document itself (not on individual days):

Title
Required - The Journey name. Single word or short phrase that captures the theme. "Light." "Silence." "Beauty, Truth & Goodness." Not a sentence.
Slug
Required - URL-safe, auto-generated from the title. e.g., "light", "silence", "beauty-truth-goodness".
Theme
Required - Reference to one of the 5 founding themes (or the intro theme). This determines filtering and where the Journey appears in the app.
Description
Required - 2–3 sentences shown on the Journey card. What will the user explore? Write it as an invitation, not a syllabus. "Seven days of following light through paint, stone, sound, and silence - and the question of what light reveals when you stop to look."
Hero Image
Required - The cover image for the Journey card. High-res, evocative. This is the first thing users see when browsing Journeys. It should capture the feeling of the theme, not illustrate it literally.
Central Question
Required (in content doc, not a Sanity field) - The question that every day's content answers from a different angle. This is the backbone of the integrated curriculum. e.g., "What does light reveal that darkness hides?" or "What do we hear when we stop talking?" Write it as something a thoughtful person with no background would genuinely want to sit with.
Estimated Minutes Per Day
Optional - Default: 10. Shown as "~10 min/day" on the Journey card.
Display Order
Optional - Controls sort order in the Journey list. Lower numbers appear first.
Published
Required - Boolean. Set to false while building. Toggle true when all 7 days are complete, all images uploaded, and content reviewed.

Journey Day Fields

Each Journey has exactly 7 days. Each day is an object within the Journey's days array with the following fields:

Day Number
Required - Integer 1–7.
Day Title
Required - A short, evocative title for the day. Not a content type label. Not "Day 3: Literature." Something that captures the encounter: "The painter who saw God's light" or "What the silence said." Lowercase unless it's a proper noun.
Opening Image
Required - The visual anchor for the entire day. Used in Step 1 (Open) and Step 3 (Breathe with 8x zoom). Minimum 1200px, ideally 3000px+ since users will zoom in. Choose images with rich detail and texture - the user will explore this image at extreme magnification.
Opening Text
Optional but strongly encouraged - 1–3 sentences for Step 1. The day's invitation. Evocative, not explanatory. See "Open" step guidance above.
Encounter Content
Required - Reference to a contentItem document. This is the main encounter for the day - the painting, the poem, the landscape, the idea. The content item has its own fields (title, description, context, image, curator note, etc.) - see the Pause & Ponder guide for Content Item field specifications. The same content item can be referenced by Pause & Ponder, Explore, and Library.
Encounter Guidance
Optional - One sentence that shapes how the user engages with the content. "Listen with your eyes closed for the first minute." "Look at the hands first. Then the face." "Read it aloud. Slowly." This is the Journey-specific editorial layer on top of the content item.
Reflection Questions
Required - 1 to 2 questions - Displayed one at a time in Step 4. Should connect the day's encounter to the user's own experience, and progressively build on the week's arc. See Reflect step guidance above.
Connect Thread
Optional - One sentence linking today to tomorrow (Step 5). Creates curiosity without spoiling. On Day 7, use a closing reflection instead - something that sends the user back into their life with the theme still echoing.
Go Deeper
Optional but strongly encouraged - Array of references to traditionReflection documents. Typically 2–3 per day. See "Go Deeper / Tradition Reflections" section below.

Go Deeper / Tradition Reflections

Each tradition reflection is a standalone Sanity document that can be linked to any Journey day. It represents one voice from the tradition - a Church Father, Saint, Pope, theologian, mystic, or Doctor of the Church - speaking to the day's content.

Author Type
Required - Currently: Church Father, Saint, Pope. Pending expansion: Theologian, Mystic, Church Doctor. Some figures hold multiple categories (e.g., St. Hildegard is both Saint and Doctor of the Church). Schema change needed: multi-select.
Title
Required - A short topical label, not the person's name. "Beauty as a path to God" or "The prayer of unknowing." This appears as the expandable bar label.
Short Quote
Optional - One memorable line. Displayed above the summary when the bar expands. Should be genuinely striking - not a generic platitude. If you can't find a quote that stops you in your tracks, leave it blank rather than using something forgettable.
Summary
Required - 2–4 sentences in plain language. This is NOT a biography. It's a plain-language explanation of what this thinker/saint/father said about the topic and why it matters. Write as if you're explaining it to a smart friend over coffee - no jargon, no academic apparatus, no assumed knowledge of Church history.
Source
Required - Full attribution. e.g., "St. Augustine, Confessions, Book X" or "Benedict XVI, Address to Artists, 2009". Include enough detail that someone could find the original.
Themes
Optional - Reference to one or more of the 5 founding themes. Helps with filtering and re-use across Journeys.
Era
Optional - Early Church/Fathers, Medieval, Modern. Useful for ensuring variety within a day's Go Deeper reflections.

Go Deeper Voice & Tone

Plain language, always. The user encountering Go Deeper may have never heard of Augustine or Aquinas. The summary must stand on its own - no assumed context. If you need to mention a concept like "the Beatific Vision," explain it in the same sentence.
Connect to the day, not just the theme. A reflection for Day 3 of the Light journey (Hopkins poem) should connect specifically to what Hopkins wrote - not just to "light" in general. The more specific the connection, the more the user feels the thread.
Variety of eras. Aim for a mix within each day: one ancient/patristic voice, one medieval, one modern. The user should feel the breadth of 2,000+ years of thinking about these questions.
Accuracy matters. Get the categories right. Meister Eckhart is not a saint. De Caussade is not a saint. Aquinas is a saint AND a Doctor of the Church. If uncertain, research before writing. A misattribution in Go Deeper undermines the entire section's credibility.
Do
"For Plato, beauty is not decorative. It is the visible form of truth itself. When you feel drawn to beauty, you are being drawn toward reality - toward what is actually true, even if you can't yet name it."
Don't
"Plato (428–348 BCE) was an ancient Greek philosopher who wrote about beauty in his dialogues, particularly the Symposium and Phaedrus. His theory of Forms posited that beauty is an ideal essence."
Do
"Hildegard didn't just talk about light - she saw it. She described visions of 'the living Light' from childhood. For her, light was not a metaphor. It was the medium through which the divine made itself known."
Don't
"St. Hildegard of Bingen (1098–1179) was a German Benedictine abbess, mystic, and Doctor of the Church. She experienced visions throughout her life and wrote extensively about them."

The Hook Rule - Every Curator Note, Every Day

Every Journey day curator note must open with one surprising, specific, shareable fact - something the user genuinely didn't know and would repeat at dinner. This is not optional. It is the non-negotiable entry point for every day's encounter.

The hook applies to Journey days exactly as it does to Pause & Ponder. The purpose is identical: give the curious seeker something specific to hold - a fact, a detail, a discovery - that changes how they see what they're about to encounter. Without a hook, the curator note defaults to atmosphere or significance claims, which give the user nothing to stand on.

Hook - What It Sounds Like
"When this triptych is closed, Bosch painted seven scenes of Christ's suffering on the outside panels in grisaille. You move through darkness to reach the Adoration."

"El Greco painted the Agony in the Garden more than once, and each time he did, the gap between Christ and the sleeping disciples gets wider."

"Rublev left an empty seat at the table - the fourth side faces the viewer. For 600 years, people have been sitting down in it."
Not a Hook
"This painting rewards slowness." - atmosphere, not information

"Bosch was one of the most visionary painters of the Northern Renaissance." - significance claim, not a fact

"This is a profound meditation on the nature of temptation." - interpretation, not discovery

The test: Would someone say "wait, I didn't know that" and repeat it at dinner? If yes: hook. If not: keep looking. The hook is always a specific fact - a detail about how the work was made, an unusual circumstance of its creation or survival, something about the artist's life that changes how the user reads the image, or a hidden element in the work itself.

For single-painting days: Look at the back of the painting. What's on the outside panels of a triptych when it's closed? What was the original context - where did it hang, who commissioned it, what did viewers know that we don't? The thing most people never see is often the best hook.

Hook by Content Type

The hook changes shape depending on what you're working with. In every case, the test is the same: would someone say "I didn't know that" and repeat it at dinner?

Content TypeWhere to Look for the Hook
Sacred ArtThe exterior panels, the original location (above an altar? in a private room?), what was painted over or lost, a hidden detail most reproductions crop out, what the original audience understood that we no longer do.
LiteratureThe circumstances of composition (written in a night, written dying, published after death), a line the author cut from the final version, what the title actually means in the original language, who the poem was written for.
MusicThe premiere reaction (booed or wept?), a deliberate structural choice the composer made that most listeners never notice, what the piece was originally written for vs. how it's now performed, a live recording moment where something went wrong and became legendary.
ThinkerThe moment that broke their thinking open, the question they spent a lifetime failing to fully answer, the thing they believed that no one else would accept, the conversation that changed everything.
LandscapeThe geological or ecological fact that changes how you read the place, what the land was used for that most visitors don't know, a physical detail only visible at a specific time of day or year.
Math / ScienceThe discovery that arrived backwards (the answer came before the proof), the number or pattern that kept appearing where it shouldn't, the scientist who was laughed at and turned out to be right.
Food / WineHow long the tradition actually is (not decades - centuries), the moment it nearly disappeared, the one ingredient or step that cannot be hurried.
Watch / ListenWhat the filmmaker or speaker originally intended to make, the moment in the piece where the argument turns, the reaction it received that no one expected.

Field Rules: Each Field Has One Job

The most common failure mode in KALLOS content writing is repetition across fields. Claude and other AI writing tools default to re-explaining the opening text in every subsequent field. The curator note summarizes the story. The context re-tells it with background. The encounter note explains what it means. By the end, the major connection has landed four times and lost all its force. Every field must do different work.

Before finalizing any day, check: does any single connection, fact, or beat appear in more than one field? If yes, cut it from all but the strongest one. The "turn" -- the unexpected connection that is the day's emotional payload -- lands exactly once, in the field where it hits hardest. Usually that is the opening text ending or the lectio connection note, never both.

FieldIts One JobWhat Does Not Belong Here
Opening TextTell the myth, story, or event fully. Start in a scene, not a thesis. End on the major turn if it belongs here.Significance claims. Theology. Explaining what the story means.
Curator NoteOne surprising fact about the artwork or artist specifically. End on the unexpected detail and stop.Retelling the opening text. Theological connections. Explaining what the fact means.
Brief DescriptionDescribe what you see in the image. 2-3 sentences. Sensory and specific.The hook. Significance. Connection to the journey theme.
ContextBackground that deepens the encounter -- historical, cultural, theological, whatever serves the day.Anything already told in the opening text or curator note. Anything another field will say better. The rule is no repetition, not content type restriction.
Encounter Note2-3 sentences. Point the user at one specific thing in the work. Give a fact that lets them feel a connection without being told what to feel.Explaining the significance. Restating the opening text. Making the theological argument explicit.
Encounter GuidanceOne sentence. Tell the user exactly where to look or what to do.Explanation. More than one instruction.
Lectio Connection NoteIf the major "turn" was held back from the opening text, this is where it lands. Short. Let the scripture speak.Over-explaining the pairing. Making a connection that already appeared in another field.

The repetition test: Read all fields in sequence. Every time you notice the same beat appearing twice, cut it from the weaker field. A connection that lands four times lands zero times.

Symbolism - Name It, Explain It, Every Time

Never assume the user knows what a symbol means. A peacock in a medieval painting, an ichthys in a fish basket, a specific flower in a poem, a recurring interval in a piece of music - if it carries meaning the average person would miss, name it and explain it in plain language, right where it appears.

Symbolism is some of the most genuinely interesting material in KALLOS content. It is also the most likely to be written over. The instinct is to write "this detail is significant" and move on. The job is to say what the detail actually means - and why the original creator put it there.

Symbolism belongs wherever it fits naturally. If it's the most surprising thing about the piece, it's the hook (Curator Note). If it's one layer among several in a painting, it belongs in the Encounter Note or, for journeys, the encounter guidance. If it's historical context about what the symbol meant to the original audience, it belongs in Context. The field is less important than the rule: when you see symbolism, name it.
Explain it in one sentence, in plain language. "The peacock in the lower left was a standard symbol of immortality in this period - its flesh was believed not to rot." "The owl perched inside the glass sphere is Bosch's recurring image for false wisdom." "The ichthys - the fish - scratched into the wicker basket is only visible in the high-resolution image, and it would have been immediately legible to any Christian viewer in the 16th century." No art-history jargon. No "polysemous signifier." Say what it meant to the people who first saw it.
Symbolism is dinner-table conversation. This is one of the places where KALLOS content earns its place. A user who learns that the halo in Byzantine icons is not decorative but represents uncreated light - that it was a theological statement about the nature of divinity, not an artistic convention - will not forget that. Find the fact. Say the fact. Trust the fact to do its work.
Symbolism Named and Explained
"The three figures around the table in Rublev's icon represent the three angels who visited Abraham - but by the 15th century, the convention had shifted. Viewers would have understood them immediately as Father, Son, and Holy Spirit. The empty place at the front edge of the table faces the viewer. It has been there for 600 years."

"The lily in Gabriel's hand in nearly every Annunciation painting is not a decorator's choice. White lilies had meant purity so consistently for so long that any viewer of the period would have read it immediately as a comment on Mary."
Symbolism Unnamed or Assumed
"The Trinitarian imagery in the icon is rich and rewarding for those who know how to look." - the user cannot look if they don't know what to look for.

"Gabriel carries the traditional symbol of his greeting." - which symbol? Traditional to whom? The user is standing in front of a painting and has no idea what you mean.

One Detail Per Field - Never Repeat the Hook

The hook lives in the Curator Note. Once. Every other field is a different door into the same room. If the same detail - a nickname, a symbol, a biographical fact, a proverb - appears in more than one field, the user reads it twice without knowing they've been here before. The painting has hundreds of objects. Use them.

Each field has a specific job. These jobs do not overlap:

FieldIts JobWhat It Is NOT
Brief DescriptionThe visual anchor on the card - what does it look like? What is the first thing the eye lands on? Scale, composition, dominant visual fact.Not the hook. Not a meaning claim. Not a biography excerpt.
Curator NoteThe hook - one surprising, specific, dinner-table fact. This is its home and only its home.Not a summary of the painting. Not an atmosphere sentence. Not repeated anywhere else.
ContextThe story behind the painting - history, commission, reception, what the original audience knew. Different territory from the hook.Not the hook restated. Not an art history lecture. Not repeated from Brief Description.
Opening TextThe emotional threshold for Step 1 - the sentence that makes the user lean in before they've seen the encounter. Mood, not information. Or a different specific detail than the Curator Note uses.Not a preview of the Curator Note. Not the hook restated. Not "Today we explore..."
Encounter NoteSymbol decoder - specific objects, visual elements, and what they meant to Bosch's original audience. Multiple symbols, none of them the one already in the Curator Note.Not the hook again. Not general context. Not biographical information.
What Repetition Looks Like - Day 4 Before Fix
Brief Description: "Early critics called this 'The Strawberry Plant.'"
Curator Note: "Early critics didn't call this 'The Garden.' They called it 'The Strawberry Plant.'"
Opening Text: "Bosch's contemporaries didn't call this 'The Garden.' They called it 'The Strawberry Plant.'"
Encounter Note: "Early critics called the entire triptych 'The Strawberry Plant.'"

The same hook four times. The painting has 200+ objects. Use them.
What It Should Look Like - Four Different Doors
Brief Description: Scale and visual fact - people dwarfed by birds, wrong animals together.
Curator Note: The hook - "They called it 'The Strawberry Plant'" lives here and only here.
Opening Text: The emotional entry - the circling procession, nobody arriving anywhere.
Encounter Note: Four distinct symbols - the owl inside the glass sphere, mussels/oysters, the animal carousel, the impossible architecture.

Before writing any field, ask: Have I used this detail - this nickname, this symbol, this biographical fact - in any other field for this day? If yes: find a different detail. The painting will have one.

Voice & Tone - The Immersive Experience

Journeys render in espresso mode - dark background, cream text, sage accents. The entire experience is designed to feel immersive and contemplative, like entering a quiet room. The writing should match.

The audience is the curious seeker. Someone moved by beauty, regardless of faith background. Assume curiosity, not faith. Never frame content as though the user is Catholic or assumed to hold a particular belief. Never use "non-Catholic" as a user category.
Assume no one knows this content. Not the artist. Not the story. Not what the symbol means. Not the thinker's name. Not why the piece matters. The user may be brilliant, widely read, and deeply curious - and still have never heard of Hildegard of Bingen, or Jean-Pierre de Caussade, or what "chiaroscuro" means, or why Gregorian chant is structured the way it is. Write for that person. Every time. If you catch yourself writing "as viewers of the period knew" without explaining what they knew - stop and explain it. The goal is dinner-table conversation, not a textbook that assumes the reader already did the reading.
No em dashes. Em dashes (the long dash) are a telltale sign of AI-generated text and are banned in all KALLOS content. Use a comma, a period, or a short hyphen instead. Rewrite the sentence if the thought needs an em dash to hold together - that means the sentence structure is wrong.
Banned words. Never use: "journey" (for the experience itself), "sacred," "profound," "transformative," "spiritual practice," "invitation to...," "encounter with the divine" (as a generic phrase), or "nourish your soul." These words have been used so often they carry no information. Find the specific thing - then say the specific thing.
No first person. Never "I," "my," or "me." The app does not refer to itself or its curator as a person. Same rule as P&P.
No app-speak. Never "Welcome to Day 3!" or "Today we'll explore..." or "Tap to continue." The user is in a contemplative state. Don't break the spell with interface language.
Short, weighted sentences. In espresso mode, text appears on a dark screen. Long paragraphs feel heavy and hard to read. Opening text should be 1–3 sentences. Encounter guidance: one sentence. Connect threads: one sentence. Let the brevity carry weight.
Earn the emotional moment. Don't start at the peak. Day 1 opening text should be warm and inviting, not dramatic. Let the drama build through the week. By Day 7, you've earned the big sentence - but only because the six days before it did the work.
Trust the content. The painting will move the user. The music will move the user. Your job is to get the user to the encounter and then get out of the way. Describe, don't explain. Frame, don't interpret. Point, don't lecture.
Never use "shaft" to describe light. Use "ray" or "stream" instead. "A shaft of light" is common to the point of invisibility and carries no information. "A ray of light through the high nave window" gives the user something to see.
Eucatastrophe must be defined inline on first use. If the word appears in any KALLOS content, define it in the same sentence: "a eucatastrophe, a sudden joyful turn after the long darkness." The second use in the same piece does not need re-definition. Do not assume the user knows Tolkien's coinage.
Never say "non-Catholic" as a user category. The audience is always "the curious seeker." Never frame KALLOS as "not a Catholic app" - that framing apologizes for something that needs no apology. The content points toward beauty, truth, and goodness. The tradition belongs to everyone.
Avoid "Surprising History" as a label. When content has a historical fact with genuine discovery power, use it. Frame it as discovery ("here's something that happened"), never apologetics ("here's why this isn't what you think"). When the fact is strong enough to open the day, it belongs in the Curator Note. When it enriches without headlining, it belongs in Context. Not every day needs one - only when the material genuinely earns it.
Journey Voice
"The monks of Lascaux had no words for what they were doing. They had firelight, mineral pigment, and a wall that had waited forty thousand years. They painted anyway."
Not Journey Voice
"Cave paintings represent some of the earliest known examples of human artistic expression. The Lascaux caves in France contain paintings dating back approximately 17,000 years."
Journey Voice
"Listen with your eyes closed for the first minute. Then open them. Notice what changed."
Not Journey Voice
"For optimal enjoyment of this musical piece, we recommend listening with headphones in a quiet environment."

Building a Journey - Step by Step

1. Start with the Central Question

Every Journey begins with a single question that is big enough to sustain seven days but specific enough to ground each day's encounter. The question should pass the curious seeker test: would someone with no religious background genuinely want to sit with this?

Good Central Questions
"What does light reveal that darkness hides?"
"What do we hear when we stop talking?"
"How does beauty survive darkness?"
"What is the world trying to tell us?"
"Why do we long for something we've never seen?"

2. Choose 7 Encounters

Select one content item per day. Each should answer the central question from a different angle using a different content type. Think about emotional sequencing - open accessible, deepen in the middle, integrate at the end.

3. Write Opening Text for Each Day

Write these in sequence. Read them back-to-back. The seven openings should feel like a single arc - distinct each day, but clearly part of the same story. If you read Day 1 and Day 7 side by side, the distance traveled should be palpable.

4. Write Reflection Questions

Start personal and concrete (Day 1–2). Build toward self-awareness and pattern recognition (Day 3–5). Close with integration - the big question the week has been building toward (Day 6–7).

5. Write Connect Threads

Write these after the encounters are finalized. Each connect thread bridges two specific encounters. The thread from Day 3 to Day 4 should only work between those two specific days - if it could go between any two days, it's too generic.

6. Write Go Deeper Reflections

Select tradition voices that specifically connect to each day's content. Aim for 2–3 per day, varied by era. Write plain-language summaries that a curious seeker with no background can understand.

7. Source Images

Opening images must be high-res (minimum 1200px, ideally 3000px+) because users will zoom to 8x on the Breathe step. Choose images with detail, texture, and depth. Content item images follow standard content item requirements (min 1200px).

Single-Artist Journeys - A Different Structure

The integrated curriculum model (one theme, seven content types) is the standard Journey structure. But there is a second valid structure: a single-artist or single-body-of-work Journey, where all seven days explore the work of one artist or one corpus across seven paintings, texts, or compositions.

In a single-artist Journey, the artist's body of work IS the content type variety. Instead of moving between painting, music, and landscape, the user moves between different works - or different aspects of the same work - by the same hand. The question changes from "how does beauty appear in different forms?" to "what is this artist saying, and why does it still matter?"

When to Use This Structure

A single-artist Journey works when: the artist's body of work is large enough and varied enough to sustain seven distinct encounters; the artist's vision is complex enough that seven days genuinely deepens rather than exhausts it; and the artist is surprising enough that most users don't already have a fixed story about them.

Arc Logic for Single-Artist Journeys

The standard three-movement arc (Arrival → Deepening → Integration) still applies, but with one important addition: start with what most people never see.

The first day of a single-artist Journey should ideally reveal something about the work that most viewers walk past - the exterior panels of a triptych when closed, a preparatory sketch, an early work before the famous style was formed, or a detail most reproductions crop out. This performs two functions: it immediately signals that this Journey will show the user things they haven't seen, and it establishes a kind of visual literacy - you will learn to look differently this week.
Example - Bosch 7-Day Journey Arc
Central Question: "Why do humans keep reaching for things that destroy them?"

Day 1 (Arrival): The closed Adoration triptych - the grisaille exterior panels showing Christ's Passion. Most people only ever see the interior. The hook: you move through suffering to reach the Adoration.
Day 2 (Arrival): The Conjurer - Bosch's commentary on self-deception. The audience watching the trick are being pickpocketed while they look away.
Days 3–5 (Deepening): Garden of Earthly Delights panels - central garden, Haywain (the hayride of greed), Hell panel detail.
Day 6 (Deepening): Temptation of Saint Anthony - Bosch's own question: how does one keep choosing the good?
Day 7 (Integration): The full Garden of Earthly Delights triptych - now the user can read it. Day 1's Lectio reference (1 Cor 13:12, "now we see through a glass darkly") returns. The arc closes.

The Return Arc

Single-artist Journeys benefit especially from a return arc: a motif, a scripture, a phrase, or an image from Day 1 that comes back in Day 7 with earned weight. The user has spent seven days inside this artist's world - they are ready to hear something again that they only half-heard at the start. Use it.

Audio Sourcing Rules (Auditio)

The goal is to keep the user in the app. Every audio reference should be a specific arrangement or performance - not an album, not a playlist, not a link to Spotify. The user should press play and hear exactly what you intended, without leaving KALLOS.

Rule 1 - Licensing First: Free, Downloadable, No Copyright

Must be free to download and use with no copyright restrictions. No Spotify, no Apple Music, no YouTube-only, no copyrighted recordings.

Approved sources (use these first):

  • Pixabay Music - Catholic - free, no copyright, direct download
  • Musopen.org - public domain classical performances
  • Free Music Archive - Creative Commons
  • Internet Archive - public domain recordings
  • gregorian-chant-hymns.com - free for personal/institutional use; attribution required (note source abbey/schola in Sanity artist field)
  • Bandcamp free downloads and artist sites offering direct downloads with explicit free-use license

Format: MP3 preferred. If only MP4/AAC is available, convert to MP3 before uploading to Sanity.

Rule 2 - Connection Can Be Thematic, Mood-Based, or Emotional

The Auditio doesn't have to match the subject literally. A piece can match the feeling of a day, the emotional arc, or the theological undercurrent.

Good thematic match
Victoria "O Vos Omnes" paired with Bosch's Adoration - matches the grief-to-joy arc of the triptych, not the subject literally.
Good direct match
The Porter's Gate, "Breastplate of Saint Patrick" for a St. Patrick feast day Journey day - matches theme, mood, and subject directly.

Rule 3 - Genre Is Open, but Non-Sacred/Classical Must Earn Its Place

Sacred and classical is the natural default. Gregorian chant, polyphony, Bach, Pärt, Palestrina - this is the home territory of KALLOS audio. Default here unless you have a strong reason to go elsewhere.
Contemporary folk, ambient, world music, and other genres are welcome if: the piece is likely new to the user, genuinely surprising in context, and strongly meets Rules 1 and 2. Do not use well-known mainstream music the user would already associate with other contexts.

Rule 4 - Specific Arrangements, Not Albums or Playlists

Link to a single performance of a single piece. "Arvo Pärt, Spiegel im Spiegel - performed by Tasmin Little and Martin Roscoe (5:42)" not "Arvo Pärt, Tabula Rasa (album)."

Rule 5 - Ready-to-Play

The ideal MP3 has clean start/end points, no long silences, and appropriate length (typically 2-8 minutes). If a track needs trimming, note it explicitly - but prefer finding a clean recording over planning to edit.

Rule 6 - Upload to Sanity, Not External Links

Use the audioFile field (Sanity-hosted) whenever possible. The musicUrl field (external link) is a last resort. Never link to YouTube, Spotify, or Apple Music - the user hits ads, paywalls, or leaves the contemplative experience.

Attribution

Note the source and performer in the Sanity artist field. For gregorian-chant-hymns.com material, add the source abbey or schola to the artist field (e.g., "St. Cecilia's Abbey, UK").

For each Journey day that includes audio, the content doc should note: (1) exact arrangement and performer, (2) source URL and license/public domain status, (3) whether the MP3 is ready to upload or needs trimming, (4) duration.

Rule 7 - Always Offer Two Options

For every Auditio suggestion, provide a primary sacred/classical option AND at least one alternative from a different register (folk, ambient, meditation, contemporary, world music, or a well-chosen secular track). Too much of the same register becomes invisible through repetition. The alternative must meet the emotional and licensing tests. Sheri chooses.
gregorian-chant-hymns.com delivers MP4 files. Always convert to MP3 before uploading to Sanity. Use ffmpeg in the Cowork VM or any audio converter. Note the source abbey/schola in the Sanity artist field.

The Lectio Pairing - Philosophy and Scripture

The editorial thesis behind every Lectio: The great philosophers were asking questions that scripture had already answered. The Lectio is the room where that conversation happens. Philosophy shows the question. Scripture provides the arrival.

Every Journey day has an optional Lectio section: a philosophy quote paired with a scripture passage. Philosophy goes first (familiar entry point for the curious seeker with no faith background), scripture second (the discovery). The user encounters the philosopher's longing, then hears the answer they were reaching toward.

The Philosophy Quote

One line only. Modern, accessible translation. The reader should feel the thought land, not work to parse it.
The quote must show a question or a longing - not a conclusion. The philosopher should sound like someone reaching for something just out of grasp. Scripture then provides what they were reaching for. If the philosophy quote already sounds like an answer, it does not belong in the Lectio.
Philosophy Showing the Question
"Beauty itself is but the sensible image of the Infinite." (Francis Jeffrey) - the philosopher sees something but cannot name where it leads.

"Our heart is restless, until it repose in Thee." (Augustine, Confessions) - longing without arrival.

"To philosophize is to learn to die." (Cicero) - the philosopher sees through the surface but cannot see the destination.
Philosophy That Is Already an Answer
A quote from a Church Father that directly names God as the answer - this belongs in Go Deeper, not the Lectio philosophy slot. The philosophy field is for secular or pre-Christian thinkers whose questions scripture answers. Church Fathers belong to the tradition, not to the longing side of the conversation.

Format rules:

  • One line or short sentence. If the quote requires more than one breath to read, it is too long.
  • Use modern, accessible translation - not archaic phrasing. The user should feel the thought, not translate it.
  • Include the thinker's name and source in the attribution field: "Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics Book I" or "Cicero, Tusculan Disputations."
  • Avoid the first-search-result choice. The obvious Plato quote about beauty, the Augustine "restless heart" line - these have been reproduced so many times they have lost their power. Keep looking unless the obvious choice is genuinely the best fit for this specific day's content.

The Scripture Passage

Scripture translation standard: RSV-2CE only. This is the Revised Standard Version, Second Catholic Edition (Ignatius Press). Literary, faithful to original languages, used by Catholic scholars. Do NOT use NIV, ESV, NABRE, or any other translation. If you are uncertain of the RSV-2CE wording, look it up before writing it.
The scripture should feel like an arrival, not an explanation. The philosopher circled the truth for a lifetime. The scripture names it directly. The pairing should produce a moment of recognition: "that is what he was looking for."

Quality Standard - This Specific Day, Not Any Day

Both the philosophy quote and the scripture must be chosen because they connect specifically to what the user just encountered in that day's content - not because they relate to the general theme. A quote that could appear in any KALLOS day is the wrong quote for this day.

The test: Does this pairing make this specific content item unlock in a new way? Does it feel like a thread the user didn't see coming? If it's interchangeable with another day, keep looking. Do not force a pairing - a strong scripture alone is better than a strained philosophy match.

Lectio That Works - Day Connected to a Specific Painting
Day with Caravaggio's "Supper at Emmaus":
Philosophy: "The eyes are useless when the mind is blind." (Epictetus, Discourses) - fits the day precisely because the disciples walked with Christ for hours without recognizing him. Epictetus points to the failure of sight without understanding. Scripture then names the moment of recognition.
Scripture: "And their eyes were opened and they recognized him; and he vanished out of their sight." (Luke 24:31, RSV-2CE)

Why it works: the philosophy is not about resurrection or goodness in general - it is about the gap between seeing and understanding, which is the exact drama of the Emmaus story.
Lectio That Fails
Same day, wrong pairing:
Philosophy: "Beauty is the splendor of truth." (Aquinas) - Augustine/Aquinas do not belong in the philosophy slot. They are already inside the tradition, not reaching toward it. And this quote relates to beauty as a theme, not to the Emmaus encounter specifically.
Scripture: "For God so loved the world..." (John 3:16) - famous, but has no specific connection to the Emmaus dinner table, the moment of recognition, or the opened eyes.

Philosophy Source (journeyDay > lectio > philosophySource)
Optional - Full attribution. e.g., "Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics Book I" or "Plato, Phaedrus 250d".

The Classical Lens - Every Day Must Pass This Test

Does this day's encounter lead the user toward beauty, truth, or goodness - without telling them that's what's happening?

Same test as Pause & Ponder, but applied across the 7-day arc. In a Journey, the three transcendentals show up at different structural levels:

TranscendentalWhere It Lives in a Journey Day
BeautyThe encounter itself (Step 2) and the image (Steps 1 & 3). The thing that stops the user mid-scroll and holds them.
TruthThe context, curator note, and Go Deeper reflections. Why this matters. What it reveals about reality. The intellectual and spiritual depth beneath the beauty.
GoodnessThe reflection questions (Step 4) and the connect thread (Step 5). The invitation to carry the encounter into the user's life. Beauty and truth made personal.

Across the 7-day arc, the Journey itself should embody the progression: the user is first awakened by beauty (Days 1–2), then led to understand truth (Days 3–5), then called toward goodness (Days 6–7). This is the classical sequence of the Transcendentals - and it should be felt, not taught.

Common Mistakes

Right Instinct
Choosing 7 encounters that answer the central question from genuinely different angles. Each day feels surprising but connected.
Wrong Instinct
Choosing 7 encounters that all feel similar. Three sacred art days in a row. Two landscapes back to back. The user feels repetition instead of discovery.
Right Instinct
Writing opening text that creates a mood. "The monks had firelight, mineral pigment, and a wall that had waited forty thousand years."
Wrong Instinct
Writing opening text that previews the content. "Today you'll encounter the cave paintings of Lascaux, which date to approximately 15,000 BCE."
Right Instinct
Writing Day 7 reflection questions that pull the whole week together. "If light reveals what darkness hides - what has this week revealed to you?"
Wrong Instinct
Writing Day 7 as if it's just another standalone day. "What did you think about today's content?" - doesn't earn its position as the final day.
Right Instinct
Go Deeper reflections that connect specifically to the day's encounter. "For Hildegard, light was not a metaphor. She described visions of 'the living Light' from childhood."
Wrong Instinct
Go Deeper reflections that are generic to the theme. "Many saints have written about the importance of light in the spiritual life."

What a Journey Is NOT

Not a course. There is no quiz. No "key takeaway." No learning objectives. The user discovers the theme through encounter, not instruction.
Not a devotion. A Journey does not assume the user prays, goes to church, or identifies as Catholic. Visio Divina ("Pray with this image") is NOT available inside Journey steps - only via Explore and Library. The Journey is pre-religious: it leads the user to the threshold. It does not push them through.
Not a reading plan. The user does not "complete" a Journey by consuming content. They complete it by sitting with seven encounters and letting the question work on them. Finishing fast is not the point. Slowing down is.
Not entertainment. The content should be genuinely beautiful, intellectually rich, and emotionally resonant. But it's not curated for clicks or engagement. It's curated for depth. If a day's content is challenging - a difficult poem, a stark landscape, a dissonant piece of music - that's fine. The user should have to sit with it. Comfort is not the goal. Encounter is.
